          <img alt="teachthought-simple-taxonomy-for-understanding" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-9175" height="567" src="/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/teachthought-simple-taxonomy-for-understanding.jpg" width="756"/>
          <strong>
           How They Get It: A New, Simple Taxonomy For Understanding
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          by
          <strong>
           Terry Heick
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          How can you tell if a student really understands something?
         </p>
         <p>
          They learn early on to fake understanding exceptionally well, and even the best assessment leaves something on the table. (In truth, a big portion of the time students simply dont know what they dont know.)
         </p>
         <p>
          The idea of understanding is, of course, at the heart of all learning, and solving it as a puzzle is one of the three pillars of formal learning environments and education.
         </p>
         <p>
          1. What do they need to understand (standards)?
         </p>
         <p>
          2. What (and how) do they currently understand (assessment)?
         </p>
         <p>
          3. How can they best come to understand what they currently do not (planning learning experiences and instruction)?
         </p>
         <p>
          But how do we know if they know it?And what is it?
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           Understanding As It
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          On the surface, there is trouble with the word it. Sounds vague. Troublesome. Uncertain. But everyone somehow knows what it is.
         </p>
         <p>
          It is essentially what is to be learned, and it can be scary thing to both teachers and students.It is everything, described with intimidating terms like objective, target, proficiency, test, exam, grade, fail, and succeed.
         </p>
         <p>
          And in terms of content, it could be almost anything: a fact, a discovery, a habit, skill, or general concept, from a mathematical theory to a scientific process, the importance of a historical figure to an authors purpose in a text.
         </p>
         <p>
          So if a student gets it, beyond pure academic performance what might they be able to do?There are many existing taxonomies and characteristics, from
          <a href="http://projects.coe.uga.edu/epltt/index.php?title=Bloom's_Taxonomy" target="_blank" title="Bloom's Taxonomy">
           Blooms
          </a>
          to
          <a href="http://www.teachthought.com/learning/5-alternatives-to-blooms-taxonomy/" target="_blank" title="6 Facets of Understanding">
           Understanding by Designs
          </a>
          <em>
           6 Facets of Understanding.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          The following actions are setup as a linear taxonomy, from most basic to the most complex. The best part about it is its simplicity: Most of these actions can be performed simply in the classroom in minutes, and dont require complex planning or an extended exam period.
         </p>
         <p>
          By using a quick diagram, concept map, t-chart, conversation, picture, or short response in a journal, quick face-to-face collaboration, on an exit slip, or via digital/social media,understanding can be evaluated in minutes, helping to replace testing and consternation with a climate of assessment. It can be even be displayed on a class website or hung in the classroom to help guide self-directed learning, with students checking themselves for understanding.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           How It Works
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          Ill write more about this soon, and also put this into a more graphic form soon, as both of these are important. For now Ill say that it can be used to guide planning, assessment, curriculum design, and self-directed learning.
         </p>
         <p>
          The taxonomy is meant to be simple, arranged as (mostly) isolated tasks that range in complexity from less to more. That said, students neednt demonstrate the highest levels of understandingthat misses the point.
          <strong>
           Any ability to complete these tasks is a demonstration of understanding.
          </strong>
          The greater number of tasks the student can complete the better, but all boxes checked are evidence of that the student gets it:36 Simple, Ranked Tasks For Students To Demonstrate Understanding.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           TeachThoughts Simple Taxonomy For Understanding
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <em>
           <strong>
            Early Understanding
           </strong>
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           1. Parts
          </strong>
         </p>
         <div>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Explain or describe it simply
            </strong>
           </li>
           <li>
            Label its major and minor parts
           </li>
           <li>
            Evaluate its most and least important characteristics
           </li>
           <li>
            Deconstruct or unbuild it efficiently
           </li>
           <li>
            Give examples and non-examples
           </li>
           <li>
            Separate it into categories, or as an item in broader categories
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           <strong>
            2. Whole
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Explain it in micro-detail and macro-context
            </strong>
           </li>
           <li>
            Create a diagram that embeds it in a self-selected context
           </li>
           <li>
            Explain how it is and is not useful both practically and intellectually
           </li>
           <li>
            Play with it casually
           </li>
           <li>
            Leverage it both in parts and in whole
           </li>
           <li>
            Revise it expertly, and explain the impact of any revisions
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           <strong>
            3. Interdependence
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Explain how it relates to similar and non-similar ideas
            </strong>
           </li>
           <li>
            Direct others in using it
           </li>
           <li>
            Explain it differentlyand preciselyto both a novice and an expert
           </li>
           <li>
            Explain exactly how and where others might misunderstand it
           </li>
           <li>
            Compare it to other similar and non-similar ideas
           </li>
           <li>
            Identify analogous but distinct ideas, concepts, or situations
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           <strong>
            4. Function
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Apply it in unfamiliar situations
            </strong>
           </li>
           <li>
            Create accurate analogies to convey its function or meaning
           </li>
           <li>
            Analyze the sweet spot of its utility
           </li>
           <li>
            Repurpose it with creativity
           </li>
           <li>
            Know
            <em>
             when
            </em>
            to use it
           </li>
           <li>
            Plausibly theorize its origins
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           <strong>
            5. Abstraction
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Insightfully or artfully demonstrate its nuance
            </strong>
           </li>
           <li>
            Criticize it in terms of what it might miss or where its dishonest
           </li>
           <li>
            Debate its truths as a supporter or devils advocate
           </li>
           <li>
            Explain its elegance or crudeness
           </li>
           <li>
            Analyze its objectivity and subjectivity, and how the two relate
           </li>
           <li>
            Design a sequel, extension, follow-up, or evolution of it
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           <strong>
            6. Self
           </strong>
          </p>
          <ol>
           <li>
            <strong>
             Self-direct future learning about the topic
            </strong>
           </li>
           <li>
            Ask specific, insightful questions about it
           </li>
           <li>
            Recall or narrate their own learning sequence or chronology (metacognition) in coming to know it
           </li>
           <li>
            Is comfortable using it across diverse contexts and circumstances
           </li>
           <li>
            Identify what they still dont understand about it
           </li>
           <li>
            Analyze changes in self-knowledge as a result of understanding
           </li>
          </ol>
          <p>
           <em>
            <strong>
             Advanced Understanding
            </strong>
           </em>
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div>
         <div>
          <em>
           Understanding by Designs 6 facets of Understanding, Blooms Taxonomy, and Marzanos New Taxonomy were also referenced in the creation of this taxonomy; ataxonomy for understanding;image attribution flickr user vincealongi
          </em>
         </div>
