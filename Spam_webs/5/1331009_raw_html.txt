     <p>
      The
      <b>
       New Culture Movement
      </b>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">
       simplified Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">
       traditional Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Xn Wnhu Yndng
       </span>
      </i>
      ) of the mid 1910s and 1920s sprang from the disillusionment with traditional Chinese culture following the failure of the Chinese Republic, founded in 1912 to address Chinas problems. Scholars like
      <a href="/wiki/Chen_Duxiu" title="Chen Duxiu">
       Chen Duxiu
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cai_Yuanpei" title="Cai Yuanpei">
       Cai Yuanpei
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Li_Dazhao" title="Li Dazhao">
       Li Dazhao
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun" title="Lu Xun">
       Lu Xun
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Zuoren" title="Zhou Zuoren">
       Zhou Zuoren
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hu_Shih" title="Hu Shih">
       Hu Shih
      </a>
      , had classical educations but began to lead a revolt against
      <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">
       Confucianism
      </a>
      . They called for the creation of a new Chinese culture based on global and
      <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">
       western
      </a>
      standards, especially
      <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
       democracy
      </a>
      and science. Younger followers took up their call for:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Vernacular literature
      </li>
      <li>
       An end to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarchal" title="Patriarchal">
        patriarchal
       </a>
       family in favor of individual freedom and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women%27s_liberation" title="Women's liberation">
        women's liberation
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       View that China is a nation among nations, not as a uniquely Confucian culture.
      </li>
      <li>
       The re-examination of Confucian texts and ancient classics using modern textual and critical methods, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Doubting_Antiquity_School" title="Doubting Antiquity School">
        Doubting Antiquity School
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Democratic and egalitarian values
      </li>
      <li>
       An orientation to the future rather than the past
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      On May 4, 1919, students in Beijing protested the
      <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Conference,_1919" title="Paris Peace Conference, 1919">
       Paris Peace Conference
      </a>
      giving German
      <a href="/wiki/Shandong_Problem" title="Shandong Problem">
       rights over Shandong
      </a>
      to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japan" title="Imperial Japan">
       Imperial Japan
      </a>
      , turning this cultural movement into a political one in what became known as the
      <a href="/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement" title="May Fourth Movement">
       May Fourth Movement
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Culture_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Two major centers of literary and intellectual activity were
      <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">
       Beijing
      </a>
       home to
      <a href="/wiki/Peking_University" title="Peking University">
       Peking University
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tsinghua_University" title="Tsinghua University">
       Tsinghua University
      </a>
       and
      <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">
       Shanghai
      </a>
      , with its flourishing publishing sector.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The founders of the New Culture Movement clustered in Peking University, where they were recruited by
      <a href="/wiki/Cai_Yuanpei" title="Cai Yuanpei">
       Cai Yuanpei
      </a>
      when he became chancellor. Chen Duxiu as dean and Li Dazhao as librarian in turn recruited leading figures such as the philosopher
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hu_Shi" title="Hu Shi">
       Hu Shi
      </a>
      , the scholar of Buddhism
      <a href="/wiki/Liang_Shuming" title="Liang Shuming">
       Liang Shuming
      </a>
      , the historian
      <a href="/wiki/Gu_Jiegang" title="Gu Jiegang">
       Gu Jiegang
      </a>
      , and many more. Chen founded the journal
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Youth" title="New Youth">
        New Youth
       </a>
      </i>
      in 1915, which became the most prominent of hundreds of new publications for the new middle class public.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Shikai" title="Yuan Shikai">
       Yuan Shikai
      </a>
      , who inherited part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">
       Qing dynasty
      </a>
      military after it collapsed in 1911, attempted to establish order and unity, but failed to protect China against Japan and in his attempt to have himself declared emperor. When he died in 1916, the collapse of the traditional order seemed complete and there was an intensified search for a replacement which would go deeper than the changes of the previous generations which brought new institutions and new political forms. Daring leaders called for a new culture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      A substantial literary establishment  publishing houses, journals, literary societies, and universities  provided a foundation for an active literary and intellectual scene over the course of the following decades. The
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Jeunesse" title="La Jeunesse">
        New Youth
       </a>
      </i>
      journal, which was a leading forum for debating the causes of China's weakness, laid the blame on Confucian culture. Chen Duxiu called for "Mr. Confucius" to be replaced by "Mr. Science" and "Mr. Democracy." Another outcome was the promotion of
      <a href="/wiki/Written_vernacular_Chinese" title="Written vernacular Chinese">
       written vernacular Chinese
      </a>
      (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ). Hu Shih proclaimed that "a dead language cannot produce a living literature." In theory, the new format allowed people with little education to read texts, articles and books. He charged that literary, or
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">
       Classical Chinese
      </a>
      , which had been the written language prior to the movement, was only understood by scholars and officials (ironically, the new vernacular included many foreign words and Japanese neologisms, which made it difficult for many to read).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Scholars such as Y.R. Chao (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhao_Yuanren" title="Zhao Yuanren">
       Zhao Yuanren
      </a>
      ) began the study of the Chinese language and dialects using tools of western
      <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
       linguistics
      </a>
      . Hu Shih was among the scholars who used the textual study of
      <a href="/wiki/Dream_of_the_Red_Chamber" title="Dream of the Red Chamber">
       Dream of the Red Chamber
      </a>
      and other vernacular fiction as the basis for the national language. Literary societies such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Moon_Society" title="Crescent Moon Society">
       Crescent Moon Society
      </a>
      flourished.
     </p>
     <p>
      The literary output of this time was huge, with many writers who later became famous (such as
      <a href="/wiki/Mao_Dun" title="Mao Dun">
       Mao Dun
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lao_She" title="Lao She">
       Lao She
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun" title="Lu Xun">
       Lu Xun
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bing_Xin" title="Bing Xin">
       Bing Xin
      </a>
      ) publishing their first works. For example, Lu Xun's essays and short fiction created a sensation with their condemnation of Confucian culture.
      <a href="/wiki/A_Madman%27s_Diary" title="A Madman's Diary">
       Diary of a Madman
      </a>
      directly implied that China's traditional culture was cannibalistic, and
      <a href="/wiki/The_True_Story_of_Ah_Q" title="The True Story of Ah Q">
       The True Story of Ah Q
      </a>
      showed the typical Chinese as weak and self-deceiving.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Along with this musicians such as
      <a href="/wiki/Yin_Zizhong" title="Yin Zizhong">
       Yin Zizhong
      </a>
      joined the movement through music.
     </p>
     <p>
      New Culture leaders and their followers now saw China as a nation among nations, not as culturally unique.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      A large number of Western doctrines became fashionable, particularly those that reinforced the cultural criticism and nation-building impulses of the movement.
      <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">
       Social Darwinism
      </a>
      , which had been influential since the late nineteenth century, was especially shaping for Lu Xun, among many others.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and was supplemented by almost every "ism" of the world. Cai Yuanpei,
      <a href="/wiki/Li_Shizeng" title="Li Shizeng">
       Li Shizeng
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Wu_Zhihui" title="Wu Zhihui">
       Wu Zhihui
      </a>
      developed a
      <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_in_China" title="Anarchism in China">
       Chinese variety of anarchism
      </a>
      . They argued that Chinese society had to undergo radical social change before political change would be meaningful.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDirlik1991_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDirlik1991-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The pragmatism of
      <a href="/wiki/John_Dewey" title="John Dewey">
       John Dewey
      </a>
      became popular, often through the work of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hu_Shi" title="Hu Shi">
       Hu Shi
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiang_Monlin" title="Chiang Monlin">
       Chiang Monlin
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Tao_Xingzhi" title="Tao Xingzhi">
       Tao Xingzhi
      </a>
      . Dewey arrived in China in 1919, and spent the following year lecturing.
      <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">
       Bertrand Russell
      </a>
      also lectured widely to warm crowds. Lu Xun was associated with the ideas of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nietzsche" title="Nietzsche">
       Nietzsche
      </a>
      , which were also propagated by
      <a href="/wiki/Li_Shicen" title="Li Shicen">
       Li Shicen
      </a>
      , Mao Dun, and many other intellectuals of the time.
     </p>
     <p>
      New Culture leaders, often under the influence of the anarchist program, promoted
      <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">
       feminism
      </a>
      , even
      <a href="/wiki/Free_love" title="Free love">
       free love
      </a>
      , as an attack on the traditional family, changing the terms in which the following generations conceived society. More specifically, the movement replaced sexuality over the traditional Chinese idea of kinship positionality. This substitution is a staple of the emerging individualistic theories that occurred during the era.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Among the feminist writers was
      <a href="/wiki/Ding_Ling" title="Ding Ling">
       Ding Ling
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_breakup_of_the_movement">
       Development and breakup of the movement
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Culture_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development and breakup of the movement">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement" title="May Fourth Movement">
       May Fourth Demonstrations
      </a>
      of 1919 initially united these leaders but soon there was a debate and falling out over the role of politics. Hu Shi, Cai Yuanpei, and other liberals urged the demonstrating students to return to the classroom, but Chen Duxiu and Li Dazhao, frustrated with the inadequacy of cultural change, urged more radical political action. They used their roles as Peking University faculty to organize Marxist study groups and the first meeting of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party" title="Chinese Communist Party">
       Chinese Communist Party
      </a>
      . Li called for "fundamental solutions," but Hu criticized this as abstract, calling for "more study of questions, less study of isms."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The younger followers who followed Li and Chen into organized politics included
      <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">
       Mao Zedong
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Other students heeded Hu Shi's call to return to their studies. The new approaches shaped scholarship for the next generation. The historian
      <a href="/wiki/Gu_Jiegang" title="Gu Jiegang">
       Gu Jiegang
      </a>
      , for instance, pioneered the application of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_History" title="New History">
       New History
      </a>
      he studied at Columbia University to classical Chinese texts in the Doubting Antiquity Movement.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Gu also inspired his students in the study of Chinese folk traditions which had been ignored or dismissed by Confucian scholars.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Education was high on the New Culture agenda. Cai Yuanpei headed a New Education Society, and university students joined the Mass Education Movement of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Yen" title="James Yen">
       James Yen
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tao_Xingzhi" title="Tao Xingzhi">
       Tao Xingzhi
      </a>
      which promoted literacy as a foundation for wider political participation
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism_and_public_opinion">
       Journalism and public opinion
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Culture_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Journalism and public opinion">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Chinese newspaper journalism was modernized in the 1920s according to international standards, thanks to the influence of the New Culture Movement. The roles of journalist and editor were professionalized and became prestigious careers. The business side gained importance and with a greater emphasis on advertising and commercial news, the main papers, especially in Shanghai, moved away from the advocacy journalism that characterized the 1911 revolutionary period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Outside the main centers the nationalism promoted in metropolitan dailies was not as distinctive as localism and culturalism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1924, Indian
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Laureate" title="Nobel Laureate">
       Nobel Laureate
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">
       Rabindranath Tagore
      </a>
      held numerous lectures in China. Tagore argued that China could encounter trouble by integrating too much western civilization into Chinese society. In spite of Tagore's efforts, western ideals were major components of the New Culture Movement. Democracy became a vital tool for those frustrated with the unstable condition of China, whereas science became a crucial instrument to discard the "darkness of ignorance and superstition."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In short, New Culture intellectuals advocated and debated a wide range of cosmopolitan solutions that included science, technology, individualism, music and democracy, leaving to the future the question of what organization or political power could carry them out. The anti-imperialist and populist violence of the mid-1920s soon overwhelmed New Culture intellectual inquiry and cultural programs.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schoppa_17-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Schoppa-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluations_and_changing_views">
       Evaluations and changing views
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Culture_Movement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evaluations and changing views">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Orthodox historians viewed the New Culture Movement as a revolutionary break with feudal thought and social practice and as the seedbed of revolutionary leaders who created the Communist Party of China and who went on to found the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">
       People's Republic of China
      </a>
      in 1949. Mao Zedong wrote that the May 4th Movement "marked a new stage in China's bourgeois-democratic revolution against imperialism and feudalism," and argued that "a powerful camp made its appearance in the bourgeois-democratic revolution, a camp consisting of the working class, the student masses and the new national bourgeoisie."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Historians in the west also saw the movement as marking a break between tradition and modernity, but in recent decades Chinese and western historians now commonly argue that the changes promoted by New Culture leaders had roots going back several generations and thus were not a sharp break with tradition, which is in any case quite varied, so much as an acceleration of earlier trends.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Research over the last fifty years also suggests that while radical Marxists were important in the New Culture Movement, there were many other influential leaders, including anarchists, conservatives, Christians, and liberals. This re-evaluation, while it does not challenge the high evaluation of the thinkers and writers of the period, does not accept their self-image as cultural revolutionaries.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Other historians further argue that
      <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">
       Mao Zedong
      </a>
      s communist revolution did not, as it claimed, fulfill the promise of New Culture and enlightenment but rather betrayed its spirit of independent expression and cosmopolitanism.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        21
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yu_Yingshi" title="Yu Yingshi">
       Yu Yingshi
      </a>
      , a student of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Confucian" title="New Confucian">
       New Confucian
      </a>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qian_Mu" title="Qian Mu">
       Qian Mu
      </a>
      , recently defended Confucian thought against the New Culture condemnation. He reasoned that in fact late imperial China had not been stagnant, irrational and isolated, conditions which would justify radical revolution, but rather that late Qing thinkers were already taking advantage of the creative potential of Confucius.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        22
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Xu_Jilin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Xu Jilin (page does not exist)">
       Xu Jilin
      </a>
      , a Shanghai intellectual who reflects present day liberal voices, agreed in effect with the orthodox view that the New Culture Movement was the root of the Chinese Revolution but valued the outcome differently. New Culture intellectuals, said Xu, saw a conflict between Nationalism and Cosmopolitanism in their struggle to find a rational patriotism. But the cosmopolitan movement of the 1920s was replaced by a new age of nationalism. Like a wild horse, Xu continued, jingoism, once unbridled, could no longer be restrained, thus laying the foundations for the eventual outcomes of the history of China during the first half of the twentieth century.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        23
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

       7 (Cambridge,: Harvard University Press, 1970). Careful study of central figure.
      </li>
      <li>
       Hayford, Charles W.,
       <i>
        To the People: James Yen and Village China
       </i>
       . New York: Columbia University Press, 1990. Early chapters describe the role of popular education in the New Culture.
      </li>
      <li>
       Lanza, Fabio,
       <i>
        Behind the Gate: Inventing Students in Beijing
       </i>
       . New York: Columbia University Press, 2010.
       <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/9780231152389">
        ISBN 978-0-231-15238-9
       </a>
       . Study of student culture and institutions during the New Culture period.
      </li>
      <li>
       Leo Ou-fan Lee,
       <i>
        Voices from the Iron House: A Study of Lu Xun
       </i>
       (Bloomington: Indiana University Press, 1987). Biography and literary analysis.
      </li>
      <li>
       Yusheng Lin,
       <i>
        The Crisis of Chinese Consciousness: Radical Antitraditionalism in the May Fourth Era
       </i>
       (Madison: University of Wisconsin Press, 1979). Early critique of the New Culture Movement as "iconoclastic."
      </li>
      <li>
       Manela, Erez.
       <i>
        The Wilsonian Moment: Self-Determination and the International Origins of Anticolonial Nationalism
       </i>
       . Oxford and New York: Oxford University Press, 2007. Describes the global influences on Chinese youth.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Meisner" title="Maurice Meisner">
        Maurice J. Meisner
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Li Ta-Chao and the Origins of Chinese Marxism
       </i>
       (Cambridge,: Harvard University Press, 1967). Intellectual biography of key leader and co-founder of Chinese Communist Party.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Rana_Mitter" title="Rana Mitter">
        Rana Mitter
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        A Bitter Revolution: China's Struggle with the Modern World
       </i>
       (Oxford; New York: Oxford University Press, 2004). Traces the fate of New Culture ideals through the rest of the century.
      </li>
      <li>
       Schwarcz, Vera.
       <i>
        The Chinese Enlightenment: Intellectuals and the Legacy of the May Fourth Movement of 1919
       </i>
       . Berkeley: University of California Press, 1986. Argues that May Fourth ideals were betrayed.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_I._Schwartz" title="Benjamin I. Schwartz">
        Schwartz, Benjamin
       </a>
       . "Themes in Intellectual History: May Fourth and After." In
       <i>
        Cambridge History of China
       </i>
       , Vol. 12, pt. 1: Republican China, 19121949, 406504. Cambridge, U.K.: Cambridge University Press, 1983. Overview of intellectual and cultural history.
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Spence" title="Jonathan Spence">
        Spence, Jonathan D.
       </a>
       <i>
        The Gate of Heavenly Peace: The Chinese and Their Revolution, 1895-1980.
       </i>
       Includes many New Culture leaders and their experience of revolution.
      </li>
      <li>
       Zarrow, Peter.
       <i>
        Anarchism and Chinese Political Culture
       </i>
       (New York: Columbia University Press, 1990).
      </li>
