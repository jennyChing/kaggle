     <p>
      The
      <i>
       <b>
        Tao Te Ching
       </b>
      </i>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <i>
       <b>
        Daodejing
       </b>
      </i>
      , or
      <i>
       <b>
        Dao De Jing
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">
       simplified Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%81%93" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BE%B7" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BB%8F" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">
       traditional Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%81%93" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BE%B7" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B6%93" title="wikt:">
        
       </a>
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Dodjng
       </span>
      </i>
      ), also simply referred to as the
      <i>
       <b>
        Laozi
       </b>
      </i>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Loz
       </span>
      </i>
      ),
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-stanford-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_classic_text" title="Chinese classic text">
       Chinese classic text
      </a>
      . According to tradition, it was written around 6th century BC by the sage
      <a href="/wiki/Laozi" title="Laozi">
       Laozi
      </a>
      (or Lao Tzu,
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
       Chinese
      </a>
      :
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ;
      <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
       pinyin
      </a>
      :
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Loz
       </span>
      </i>
      , literally meaning "Old Master"), a record-keeper at the
      <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">
       Zhou dynasty
      </a>
      court, by whose name the text is known in China. The text's true authorship and date of composition or compilation are still debated,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        4
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      although the oldest excavated text dates back to the late 4th century BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-stanford-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The text, as well as with the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zhuangzi_(book)" title="Zhuangzi (book)">
        Zhuangzi
       </a>
      </i>
      , is a fundamental text for both philosophical and religious
      <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
       Taoism
      </a>
      , and strongly influenced other schools, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Legalism_(Chinese_philosophy)" title="Legalism (Chinese philosophy)">
       Legalism
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">
       Confucianism
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">
       Chinese Buddhism
      </a>
      , which when first introduced into China was largely interpreted through the use of Daoist words and concepts. Many Chinese artists, including
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_poetry" title="Chinese poetry">
       poets
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_painting" title="Chinese painting">
       painters
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">
       calligraphers
      </a>
      , and even
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_garden" title="Chinese garden">
       gardeners
      </a>
      , have used the
      <i>
       Daodejing
      </i>
      as a source of inspiration. Its influence has also spread widely outside East Asia, and is among the most translated works in world literature.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-stanford-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="WadeGiles">
       WadeGiles
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">
       romanization
      </a>
      "
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      " dates back to early English transliterations in the late 19th century; its influence can be seen in words and phrases that have become well established in English. "
      <i>
       Daodejing
      </i>
      " is the

       Text
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      has a long and complex textual history. On one hand, there are transmitted versions and commentaries that date back two millennia; on the other, there are ancient bamboo, silk, and paper manuscripts that archeologists discovered in the last century.

     <p>
      There are many possible translations of the book's title:
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">
        Do/tao
       </a>
      </i>
      literally means "way", or one of its synonyms, but was extended to mean "the Way." This term, which was variously used by other Chinese philosophers (including
      <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">
       Confucius
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mencius" title="Mencius">
       Mencius
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mozi" title="Mozi">
       Mozi
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanfeizi" title="Hanfeizi">
       Hanfeizi
      </a>
      ), has special meaning within the context of Taoism, where it implies the essential, unnamable process of the universe.
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/De_(Chinese)" title="De (Chinese)">
        D/te
       </a>
      </i>
      means "virtue", "personal character," "inner strength" (virtuosity), or "integrity." The semantics of this Chinese word resemble English
      <i>
       virtue,
      </i>
      which developed from
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Virt%C3%B9" title="Virt">
        virt
       </a>
      </i>
      , a now-archaic sense of "inner potency" or "divine power" (as in "healing virtue of a drug") to the modern meaning of "moral excellence" or "goodness." Compare the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_word" title="Compound word">
       compound word
      </a>
      <i>
       taote
      </i>
      (Chinese:
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ; pinyin:
      <i>
       <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
        Dod
       </span>
      </i>
      ; literally: "ethics", "ethical principles," "morals," or "morality").
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Jng/ching
      </i>
      as it is used here means "canon," "great book," or "classic."
     </p>
     <p>
      Thus,
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      can be translated as "The Classic of the Virtuous Way."
     </p>
     <p>
      The title
      <i>
       <b>
        Daodejing
       </b>
      </i>
      is an honorific given by posterity, other titles include the amalgam
      <i>
       Loz Dodjng
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="zh-hans" xml:lang="zh-hans">
       
      </span>
      ), the honorific
      <i>
       Daode Zhen Jing
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="zh-hans" xml:lang="zh-hans">
       
      </span>
      "True Classic of the Way and the Power"), and the
      <i>
       Wuqian wen
      </i>
      (
      <span lang="zh-hans" xml:lang="zh-hans">
       
      </span>
      "Five thousand character [classic]").
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_structure">
       Internal structure
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal structure">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The received
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      is a short text of around 5,000
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">
       Chinese characters
      </a>
      in 81 brief chapters or sections (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ). There is some evidence that the chapter divisions were later additionsfor commentary, or as aids to rote memorizationand that the original text was more fluidly organized. It has two parts, the
      <i>
       Tao Ching
      </i>
      (; chaps. 137) and the
      <i>
       Te Ching
      </i>
      (; chaps. 3881), which may have been edited together into the received text, possibly reversed from an original "Te Tao Ching." The written style is laconic, has few
      <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">
       grammatical particles
      </a>
      , and encourages varied, even contradictory interpretations. The ideas are singular; the style poetic. The rhetorical style combines two major strategies: short, declarative statements and intentional contradictions. The first of these strategies creates memorable phrases, while the second forces us to create our own reconciliations of the supposed contradictions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Chinese characters in the original versions were probably written in
      <i>
       zhunsh
      </i>
      (
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AF%86" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9B%B8" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">
       seal script
      </a>
      ), while later versions were written in
      <i>
       lsh
      </i>
      (
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9A%B8" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9B%B8" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Clerical_script" title="Clerical script">
       clerical script
      </a>
      ) and
      <i>
       kish
      </i>
      (
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%A5%B7" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9B%B8" title="wikt:">
       
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Regular_script" title="Regular script">
       regular script
      </a>
      ) styles.
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.edepot.com/taocalig.html" rel="nofollow">
       Daoist Chinese Characters
      </a>
      contains a good summary of these different calligraphies.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_authenticity_of_the_author">
       Historical authenticity of the author
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical authenticity of the author">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      is ascribed to
      <a href="/wiki/Laozi" title="Laozi">
       Laozi
      </a>
      , whose historical existence has been a matter of scholastic debate. His name, which means "Old Master", has only fueled controversy on this issue. (Kaltenmark 1969:10).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:190px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laozi.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="244" data-file-width="188" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fd/Laozi.jpg" width="188"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laozi.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a href="/wiki/Laozi" title="Laozi">
         Laozi
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The first reliable reference to Laozi is his "biography" in
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">
        Shiji
       </a>
      </i>
      (63, tr. Chan 1963:3537), by Chinese historian
      <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">
       Sima Qian
      </a>
      (ca. 14586 BC), which combines three stories. First, Laozi was a contemporary of
      <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">
       Confucius
      </a>
      (551479 BC). His surname was Li (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      "plum"), and his personal name was Er (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      "ear") or Dan (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      "long ear"). He was an official in the imperial archives, and wrote a book in two parts before departing to the West. Second, Laozi was Lao Laizi (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      "Old Come Master"), also a contemporary of Confucius, who wrote a book in 15 parts. Third, Laozi was the Grand Historian and astrologer Lao Dan (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      "Old Long-ears"), who lived during the reign (384362 BC) of Duke Xian () of
      <a href="/wiki/Qin_(state)" title="Qin (state)">
       Qin)
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Generations of scholars have debated the historicity of Laozi and the dating of the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching.
      </i>
      Linguistic studies of the text's vocabulary and
      <a href="/wiki/Rhyme" title="Rhyme">
       rhyme
      </a>
      scheme point to a date of composition after the
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shi_Jing" title="Shi Jing">
        Shi Jing
       </a>
      </i>
      yet before the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Zhuangzi_(book)" title="Zhuangzi (book)">
        Zhuangzi
       </a>
      </i>
      . Legends claim variously that Laozi was "born old"; that he lived for 996 years, with twelve previous incarnations starting around the time of the Three Sovereigns before the thirteenth as Laozi. Some Western scholars have expressed doubts over Laozi's historical existence, claiming that the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      is actually a collection of the work of various authors.
     </p>
     <p>
      Many Taoists venerate Laozi as
      <i>
       Daotsu
      </i>
      the founder of the school of Dao, the
      <i>
       Daode Tianjun
      </i>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Three_Pure_Ones" title="Three Pure Ones">
       Three Pure Ones
      </a>
      , one of the eight elders transformed from
      <a href="/wiki/Taiji_(philosophy)" title="Taiji (philosophy)">
       Taiji
      </a>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_creation_myth" title="Chinese creation myth">
       Chinese creation myth
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_versions">
       Principal versions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Principal versions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Among the many transmitted editions of the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      text, the three primary ones are named after early commentaries. The "Yan Zun Version," which is only extant for the
      <i>
       Te Ching
      </i>
      , derives from a commentary attributed to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">
       Han Dynasty
      </a>
      scholar Yan Zun (, fl. 80 BC-10 AD). The "Heshang Gong Version" is named after the legendary Heshang Gong (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      "Riverside Sage") who supposedly lived during the reign (202157 BC) of
      <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wen_of_Han" title="Emperor Wen of Han">
       Emperor Wen of Han
      </a>
      . This commentary (tr. Erkes 1950) has a preface written by
      <a href="/wiki/Ge_Xuan" title="Ge Xuan">
       Ge Xuan
      </a>
      (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      , 164244 AD), granduncle of
      <a href="/wiki/Ge_Hong" title="Ge Hong">
       Ge Hong
      </a>
      , and scholarship dates this version to around the 3rd century AD. The "Wang Bi Version" has more verifiable origins than either of the above.
      <a href="/wiki/Wang_Bi" title="Wang Bi">
       Wang Bi
      </a>
      (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      , 226249 AD) was a famous
      <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms" title="Three Kingdoms">
       Three Kingdoms
      </a>
      period philosopher and commentator on the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      (tr. Lin 1977, Rump and Chan 1979) and the
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/I_Ching" title="I Ching">
        I Ching
       </a>
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      scholarship has lately advanced from archeological discoveries of manuscripts, some of which are older than any of the received texts. Beginning in the 1920s and 1930s,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marc_Aurel_Stein" title="Marc Aurel Stein">
       Marc Aurel Stein
      </a>
      and others found thousands of scrolls in the
      <a href="/wiki/Mogao_Caves" title="Mogao Caves">
       Mogao Caves
      </a>
      near
      <a href="/wiki/Dunhuang" title="Dunhuang">
       Dunhuang
      </a>
      . They included more than 50 partial and complete "Tao Te Ching" manuscripts. One written by the scribe So/Su Dan () is dated 270 AD and corresponds closely with the Heshang Gong version. Another partial manuscript has the Xiang'er () commentary, which had previously been lost.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mawangdui_and_Guodian_texts">
       Mawangdui and Guodian texts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mawangdui and Guodian texts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In 1973, archeologists discovered copies of early Chinese books, known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Mawangdui_Silk_Texts" title="Mawangdui Silk Texts">
       Mawangdui Silk Texts
      </a>
      , in a tomb dating from 168 BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-stanford-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They included two nearly complete copies of the text, referred to as Text A (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ) and Text B (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ), both of which reverse the traditional ordering and put the
      <i>
       Te Ching
      </i>
      section before the
      <i>
       Tao Ching,
      </i>
      which is why the Henricks translation of them is named "Te-Tao Ching". Based on calligraphic styles and imperial
      <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">
       naming taboo
      </a>
      avoidances, scholars believe that Text A can be dated to about the first decade and Text B to about the third decade of the 2nd century BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1993, the oldest known version of the text, written on bamboo tablets, was found in a tomb near the town of Guodian (
      <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
       
      </span>
      ) in
      <a href="/wiki/Jingmen" title="Jingmen">
       Jingmen
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">
       Hubei
      </a>
      , and dated prior to 300 BC.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stanford_2-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-stanford-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Guodian_Chu_Slips" title="Guodian Chu Slips">
       Guodian Chu Slips
      </a>
      comprise about 800 slips of bamboo with a total of over 13,000 characters, about 2,000 of which correspond with the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      , including 14 previously unknown verses.
     </p>
     <p>
      Both the Mawangdui and Guodian versions are generally consistent with the received texts, excepting differences in chapter sequence and graphic variants. Several recent
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      translations (e.g., Lau 1989, Henricks 1989, Mair 1990, Henricks 2000, Allan and Williams 2000, and Roberts 2004) utilize these two versions, sometimes with the verses reordered to synthesize the new finds.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Written_style">
       Written style
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Written style">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      was originally written in
      <a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">
       zhunsh
      </a>
      calligraphy style. It is difficult to obtain modern replicas of these styles except through specialty stores. Most modern versions use the newspaper print style
      <a href="/wiki/Regular_script" title="Regular script">
       kish
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation_and_themes">
       Interpretation and themes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interpretation and themes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The passages are ambiguous, and topics range from political advice for rulers to practical wisdom for people. Because the variety of interpretation is virtually limitless, not only for different people but for the same person over time, readers do well to avoid making claims of objectivity or superiority. Also, since the book is 81 short poems, there is little need for an abridgement.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Ineffability_of_genesis">
       Ineffability of genesis
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ineffability of genesis">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The Way that can be told of is not an unvarying way;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The names that can be named are not unvarying names.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       It was from the Nameless that Heaven and Earth sprang;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The named is but the mother that rears the ten thousand creatures, each after its kind. (chap. 1, tr. Waley)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      These famous first lines of the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      state that the Tao is
      <a href="/wiki/Ineffability" title="Ineffability">
       ineffable
      </a>
      , i.e., the Tao is nameless, goes beyond distinctions, and transcends language. However this first verse does not occur in the earliest known version from the
      <a href="/wiki/Guodian_Chu_Slips" title="Guodian Chu Slips">
       Guodian Chu Slips
      </a>
      and there is speculation that it may have been added by later commentators.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In Laozi's
      <a href="/wiki/Qingjing_Jing" title="Qingjing Jing">
       Qingjing Jing
      </a>
      (verse 1-8) he clarified the term Tao was nominated as he was trying to describe a state of existence before it happened and before time or space.
      <i>
       Way
      </i>
      or
      <i>
       path
      </i>
      happened to be the side meaning of Tao, ineffability would be just poetic. This is the
      <a href="/wiki/Chinese_creation_myth" title="Chinese creation myth">
       Chinese creation myth
      </a>
      from the primordial Tao.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Mysterious_female">
       Mysterious female
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mysterious female">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       The Valley Spirit never dies
      </dd>
      <dd>
       It is named the Mysterious Female.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       And the doorway of the Mysterious Female
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Is the base from which Heaven and Earth sprang.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       It is there within us all the while;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Draw upon it as you will; it never runs dry. (chap. 6, tr. Waley)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Like the above description of the ineffable Tao as "the mother that rears the ten thousand creatures", the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      advocates "female" (or
      <i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yin-Yang" title="Yin-Yang">
        Yin
       </a>
      </i>
      ) values, emphasizing the passive, solid, and quiescent qualities of nature (which is opposed to the active and energetic), and "having without possessing". Waley's translation can also be understood as the
      <i>
       Esoteric Feminine
      </i>
      in that it can be known intuitively, that must be complemented by the masculine, "male" (or
      <i>
       Yang
      </i>
      ), again amplified in
      <a href="/wiki/Qingjing_Jing" title="Qingjing Jing">
       Qingjing Jing
      </a>
      (verse 9-13). Yin and Yang should be balanced, "Know masculinity, Maintain femininity, and be a ravine for all under heaven." (chap. 28, tr. Mair)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Returning_.28union_with_the_Primordial.29">
       Returning (union with the Primordial)
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Returning (union with the Primordial)">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       In Tao the only motion is returning.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       The only useful quality, weakness.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       For though all creatures under heaven are the products of Being,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Being itself is the product of Not-being. " (chap. 40, tr. Waley)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Another theme is the
      <a href="/wiki/Eternal_return" title="Eternal return">
       eternal return
      </a>
      , or what Mair (1990:139) calls "the continual return of the myriad creatures to the cosmic principle from which they arose."
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a contrast between the rigidity of death and the weakness of life: "When he is born, man is soft and weak; in death he becomes stiff and hard. The ten thousand creatures and all plants and trees while they are alive are supple and soft, but when dead they become brittle and dry." (chap. 76, tr. Waley). This is returning to the beginning of things, or to one's own childhood.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      focuses upon the beginnings of society, and describes a golden age in the past, comparable with the ideas of
      <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">
       Jean-Jacques Rousseau
      </a>
      . Human problems arose from the "invention" of culture and civilization. In this idealized past, the people should have no use for any form of writing save knotted ropes, should be contented with their food, pleased with their clothing, satisfied with their homes, should take pleasure in their rustic tasks." (chap. 80, tr. Waley)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Emptiness">
       Emptiness
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Emptiness">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       We put thirty spokes together and call it a wheel;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       But it is on the space where there is nothing that the usefulness of the wheel depends.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       We turn clay to make a vessel;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       But it is on the space where there is nothing that the usefulness of the vessel depends.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       We pierce doors and windows to make a house;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       And it is on these spaces where there is nothing that the usefulness of the house depends.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Therefore just as we take advantage of what is, we should recognize the usefulness of what is not. (chap. 11, tr. Waley)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Philosophical vacuity is a common theme among Asian philosophical traditions including Taoism (especially
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Wu_wei" title="Wu wei">
        Wu wei
       </a>
      </i>
      "effortless action"),
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      , and some aspects of
      <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">
       Confucianism
      </a>
      . One could interpret the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      as a suite of variations on the "Powers of Nothingness". This predates the Buddhist
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shunyata" title="Shunyata">
       Shunyata
      </a>
      philosophy of "form is emptiness, emptiness is form" by half a millennium.
     </p>
     <p>
      Looking at a traditional Chinese
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landscape_art" title="Landscape art">
       Landscape
      </a>
      , one can understand how emptiness (the unpainted) has the power of animating the trees, mountains, and rivers it surrounds. Emptiness can mean having no fixed preconceptions, preferences, intentions, or agenda. Since "The Sage has no heart of his own; He uses the heart of the people as his heart." (chap. 49, tr. Waley). From a ruler's point of view, it is a
      <i>
       <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">
        laissez-faire
       </a>
      </i>
      approach:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       So a wise leader may say:
      </dd>
      <dd>
       "I practice inaction, and the people look after themselves."
      </dd>
      <dd>
       But from the Sage it is so hard at any price to get a single word
      </dd>
      <dd>
       That when his task is accomplished, his work done,
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Throughout the country every one says: It happened of its own accord. (chap. 17, tr. Waley)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Knowledge_and_humility">
       Knowledge and humility
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Knowledge and humility">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Knowing others is wisdom;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Knowing the self is enlightenment.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Mastering others requires force;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Mastering the self requires strength;
      </dd>
      <dd>
       He who knows he has enough is rich.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       Perseverance is a sign of will power.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       He who stays where he is endures.
      </dd>
      <dd>
       To die but not to perish is to be eternally present. (chap. 33, tr. Feng and English)
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      praises self-gained knowledge with emphasis on that knowledge being gained with humility. When what one person has experienced is put into words and transmitted to others, so doing risks giving unwarranted status to what inevitably must have had a subjective tinge. Moreover, it will be subjected to another layer of interpretation and subjectivity when read and learned by others. This kind of knowledge (or "book learning"), like desire, should be diminished. "It was when intelligence and knowledge appeared that the Great Artifice began." (chap. 18, tr. Waley) And so, "The pursuit of learning is to increase day after day. The pursuit of Tao is to decrease the doing of the self day after day." (chap. 48, tr. W. T. Chan)
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations_in_relation_to_religious_traditions">
       Interpretations in relation to religious traditions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Interpretations in relation to religious traditions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The relation between Taoism and
      <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
       Buddhism
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Chan_Buddhism" title="Chan Buddhism">
       Chan Buddhism
      </a>
      is complex and fertile. Similarly, the relationship between Taoism and
      <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">
       Confucianism
      </a>
      is richly interwoven, historically.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">
       Translations
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Translations">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      has been translated into Western languages over 250 times, mostly to English, German, and French.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to Holmes Welch, "It is a famous puzzle which everyone would like to feel he had solved."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Many translations are written by people with a foundation in Chinese language and philosophy who are trying to render the original meaning of the text as faithfully as possible into English. Some of the more popular translations are written from a less scholarly perspective, giving an individual author's interpretation. Critics of these versions, such as Taoism scholar Eugene Eoyang, claim that translators like
      <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Mitchell_(translator)" title="Stephen Mitchell (translator)">
       Stephen Mitchell
      </a>
      produce readings of the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      that deviate from the text and are incompatible with the history of Chinese thought.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Russell Kirkland goes further to argue that these versions are based on Western
      <a href="/wiki/Orientalism" title="Orientalism">
       Orientalist
      </a>
      fantasies, and represent the colonial appropriation of Chinese culture.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      In contrast,
      <a href="/wiki/Huston_Smith" title="Huston Smith">
       Huston Smith
      </a>
      , scholar of world religions, said of the Mitchell version, "This translation comes as close to being definitive for our time as any I can imagine. It embodies the virtues its translator credits to the Chinese original: a gemlike lucidity that is radiant with humor, grace, largeheartedness, and deep wisdom." Other Taoism scholars, such as Michael LaFargue
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      and Jonathan Herman,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      argue that while they are poor scholarship they meet a real spiritual need in the West. These Westernized versions aim to make the wisdom of the Tao Te Ching more accessible to modern English-speaking readers by, typically, employing more familiar cultural and temporal references.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Translational_difficulties">
       Translational difficulties
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tao_Te_Ching&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Translational difficulties">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      is written in
      <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">
       Classical Chinese
      </a>
      , which can be difficult to understand completely. Classical Chinese relies heavily on
      <a href="/wiki/Allusion" title="Allusion">
       allusion
      </a>
      to a
      <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">
       corpus
      </a>
      of standard literary works to convey
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">
       semantic
      </a>
      meaning, nuance, and
      <a href="/wiki/Subtext" title="Subtext">
       subtext
      </a>
      . This corpus was memorized by highly educated people in Laozi's time, and the allusions were reinforced through common use in writing, but few people today have this type of deep acquaintance with ancient Chinese literature. Thus, many levels of subtext are potentially lost on modern translators. Furthermore, many of the words that the
      <i>
       Tao Te Ching
      </i>
      uses are deliberately vague and ambiguous.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since there are no
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punctuation_marks" title="Punctuation marks">
       punctuation marks
      </a>
      in Classical Chinese, it can be difficult to conclusively determine where one sentence ends and the next begins. Moving a full-stop a few words forward or back or inserting a comma can profoundly alter the meaning of many passages, and such divisions and meanings must be determined by the translator. Some editors and translators argue that the received text is so corrupted (from originally being written on one-line bamboo strips linked with silk threads) that it is impossible to understand some chapters without moving sequences of characters from one place to another.
     </p>
