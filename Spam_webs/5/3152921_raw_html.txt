              <h1 class="post-title entry-title">
               Laugh and the World Laughs With You
              </h1>
              <div class="post-meta the-icons pmeta-alt">
               <span class="post-author vcard">
                By
                <a href="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/no-family-madder/author/ptracey/" rel="author" style="text-decoration:none;">
                 Patrick Tracey
                </a>
                <br/>
                ~ 2 min read
               </span>
              </div>
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div class="post-content">
              <div class="entry-content">
               <p>
                <a href="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/no-family-madder/files/2014/01/Comedy-Club-Mike.jpg">
                 <img alt="Comedy Club Mike" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-1512" height="149" src="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/no-family-madder/files/2014/01/Comedy-Club-Mike-225x149.jpg" width="225"/>
                </a>
                British researchers have tested the hypothesis that comedians might resemble other creative individuals in showing signs of madnessand they do, as it turns out.
               </p>
               <p>
                The research, published in
                <a href="http://bjp.rcpsych.org/">
                 TheBritish Journal of Psychiatry
                </a>
                , identifies aset of traits shared by creative types (comedians most sharply) and the insane. It doesnt tell us much about clinical psychosis,but ithelps explain the fine line between madness and comedy. The traits are:
                <span id="more-1509">
                </span>
               </p>
               <div align="center">
                <div id="mj_adblock">
                 <script type="text/javascript">
                  //&lt;![CDATA[ 
 OAS_RICH('Right2'); 
 //]]&gt;
                 </script>
                </div>
               </div>
               <p>
               </p>
               <p>
                <a href="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/no-family-madder/files/2014/01/Sad-Clown-Two.jpg">
                 <img alt="Sad Clown Two" class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-1513" height="300" src="http://blogs.psychcentral.com/no-family-madder/files/2014/01/Sad-Clown-Two-225x352.jpg" width="191"/>
                </a>
               </p>
               <ul>
                <li>
                 Unusual experiences (belief in telepathy and paranormal events)
                </li>
                <li>
                 Cognitive disorganization (distractibility and difficulty in focusing thoughts
                </li>
                <li>
                 Introvertive anhedonia (reduced ability to feel social and physical pleasure, including an avoidance of intimacy)
                </li>
                <li>
                 Impulsive non-conformity (tendency towards impulsive, antisocial behavior).
                </li>
               </ul>
               <p>
                The creative elements needed to produce humor are strikingly similar to those characterizing the cognitive style of people with psychosis  both schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, said Gordon Claridge, one of three co-authors of the study.
               </p>
               <p>
                Claridge, of the University of Oxfords Department of Experimental Psychology, stressed that nocorrelationwas foundbetween comic genius and mental illness, only an association between the thinking styles.
               </p>
               <p>
                While schizophrenia can be very disabling, it gives rise to some veryunusual thinking. Manicnotions may help people make spectacularly original connections as well.
               </p>
               <p>
                Although schizophrenic psychosis itself can be detrimental to humor, in its lesser form it can increase peoples ability to associate odd or unusual things or to think outside the box, Claridge explains.
               </p>
               <div align="center">
                <div id="mj_adblock">
                 <script type="text/javascript">
                  //&lt;![CDATA[ 
 OAS_RICH('x02'); 
 //]]&gt;
                 </script>
                </div>
               </div>
               <p>
                Equally, manic thinking, which is common in people with bipolar disorder, may help people combine ideas to form original and humorous connection.
               </p>
               <p>
                If mental illness is a price we pay forcreativity, the authors underscore that creative genius isfound onlyamong multitudes otherwise disabled by severe mental illness.
               </p>
               <p>
                Among them are the likes of such towering figures as Charles Buddy Bolen. The New Orleans clarinetist with a diagnosis of schizophrenia is creditedwith inventing the jazz form. No small contribution there.
               </p>
               <p>
                Yet the Silvia Plath syndrome, as its come to be known,captures the public imagination more than it probably should, no doubt because ithelps explaintwo great mysteries in one:madness and creativity.
               </p>
               <p>
                From what Ive seen, the creative plank tends to teeter somewherebetween manic depression and schizoaffective disorder.
               </p>
               <p>
                Trust me on this. Coming from a family thats both very funny and very psychotic, Ive witnessed the full spectrum, there and back again.
               </p>
               <p>
                It must come fromour father, who was bothgenuinely hilarious andslightly manic,a mix that left us all with ticklish funny bones.
               </p>
               <p>
                Good and bad, Dad left his mark on me and the sisters. On the down side, we are so used to laughing it up that we almost die of boredom around stodgy people.
               </p>
               <p>
                If that sounds horrible, its no great hardship. You meet so many stiffs in life, you learn to swerve quickly.
               </p>
               <p>
                Kidding, of course. Just trying out my comedy routine on you. Because madness doesnt just run in our family, it gallops!
               </p>
               <p>
                Baddaboom, baddabing.
               </p>
               <p>
                Maybe I should try my hand at comedy instead of blogging in vain about mental illness. Or maybe not.
               </p>
               <p>
                What the bard said remains very true:
                <em>
                 laugh and world laughs with you; cry and you cry alone
                </em>
                .
               </p>
               <p>
                Still, and even on the best of days, the divinecomedycan feelmorelikecolossal blunder.
               </p>
               <p>
                Not to worry because were never alone. Since one out of every five people is suffering from a mental illness if youretalking to four friends and theyre all fine, then its you.
               </p>
               <p>
                Maybe Ive already crossed the line. When it comes to family madness, humor requires a peculiar mental arithmetic thats laudable when done right, awful when it lands badly.
               </p>
               <p>
                Laughter,they say, may be the best medicine. Unless its not funny. Then, they say, you may need some medicine.
               </p>
