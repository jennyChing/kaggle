               <h2>
                Blood Circulation
               </h2>
               <hr/>
               Blood is pumped throughout the body by way of two different types of blood vessels: arteries and veins. Arteries carry oxygen rich blood from the heart to the rest of the body and veins return oxygen-depleted blood from the body back to the heart. The main artery that carries blood away from the heart is called the Aorta. To maintain tissue and organ health, blood traveling through arteries must reach every body cell to supply oxygen and nutrients. In exchange, body tissues release carbon dioxide and waste products that are carried by veins against the force of gravity back to the heart to become re-oxygenated and the cycle begins again.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>

               <h2>
                Blood Vessels
               </h2>
               <hr/>
               The human body is composed of three different types of vessels: arteries, veins and capillaries. Arteries are larger and thicker in structure to withstand the the force of blood from the heart. They can expand to accept a large volume of blood and contract to squeeze blood to the veins when the heart relaxes. Arteries branch into smaller vessels called Arterioles and eventually become a very thin, web-like grouping of vessels called Capillaries. It is within these vessels where the exchange of gases and nutrients occur between the blood and tissues of the body. As blood flow then begins its journey back up to the heart, capillaries begin to thicken and merge into Venules, eventually becoming Veins. Because the flow of blood back up to the heart must travel against the force of gravity, valves within the veins open and close to prevent "pooling" or back flow of blood to the lower extremities.
               <a href="vascular-conditions/varicose-veins/" rel="self" title="VARICOSE VEINS">
                Varicose veins
               </a>
               is a common vascular condition that develops as a result of valve damage or malfunction.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>

               <h2>
                Veins
               </h2>
               <hr/>
               <u>
                There are four types of veins that deliver blood from body regions to the heart:
               </u>
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Pulmonary Veins
               </strong>
               carry oxygenated blood (of which we breathe in as air) to the left side of the heart which is then carried throughout the body.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Systemic Veins
               </strong>
               return deoxygenated, carbon-dioxide rich blood from the body to the right side of the heart and is carried to the lungs where waste gas is released through exhalation.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Superficial Veins
               </strong>
               are positioned close to the skin's surface.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Deep Veins
               </strong>
               lie deep within muscle tissue.
               <br/>
               <br/>
              </div>

               <h2>
                Vascular Structure
               </h2>
               <hr/>
               <u>
                Blood vessels consist of three layers:
               </u>
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Tunica Adventitia
               </strong>
               is the strong outer covering of arteries and veins. It is composed of connective tissue, collagen and elastic fibers to stretch and accommodate fluctuations in blood pressure.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Tunica Media
               </strong>
               is the middle layer of arteries and veins. It's smooth muscle and elastic fiber composition maintains vessel strength and stability.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <strong>
                Tunica Intima
               </strong>
               is the inner layer of arteries and veins. In certain veins, the tunica intima contains valves to maintain blood flow in a single direction.
