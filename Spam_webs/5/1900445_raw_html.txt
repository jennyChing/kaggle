     <h1>
      Letting Go of Emotional Baggage when Traveling with your Parents
     </h1>
     <span>
      By
      <a href="http://travelwithagingparents.com/author/val/" rel="author" title="Posts by Valerie Grubb">
       Valerie Grubb
      </a>
      , April 1, 2014, In
      <a href="http://travelwithagingparents.com/category/tripplanning/health-2/" rel="category tag">
       Health/Medical/Safety Concerns
      </a>
     </span>

     Letting Go of Emotional Baggage when Traveling with your Parents
    </span>
   </div>
  </div>
  <div id="wrapper">
   <div id="main">
    <div id="post">
     <img alt="emotional baggage #2" class="post-thumb wp-post-image" height="300" src="http://travelwithagingparents.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/emotional-baggage-2.jpg" width="610"/>
     <div class="post-content">
      <p>
       When I tell people how much Ive traveled with my mom (300,000+ miles so farand counting!), I usually hear a shocked response along the lines of How could you spend that much time with your mom? Even people who get along great with their parents are often aghast at the thought of traveling with them.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now I wont kid you: traveling with Mom can be hard. After all, shes
       <i>
        still
       </i>
       my motherand she
       <i>
        still
       </i>
       knows how to push my buttons (and I know how to push hers)! That said, Mom and I both love to travel, and outside of a few momentary conflicts, we truly enjoy each others company.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="http://travelwithagingparents.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/emotional-baggage-claim.jpg">
        <img alt="emotional baggage claim" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-1909" height="312" src="http://travelwithagingparents.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/03/emotional-baggage-claim.jpg" width="320"/>
       </a>
       Here are six tips that I hope will help you get the most from your experience when you travel with a parent:
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        1.   Let go of your hot buttons.
       </b>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       When traveling with a parent, youll spend a lot of time in close quarters together, and aspects of their personalities (and your own) can burst forth. We all have hot buttons, and because of our shared history family members are the ones who best know how to push them (both intentionally and unintentionally).
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       When your buttons are pushed, resist the urge to fight. Just let it golet go of being right, of having the last word, of proving how smart you are. After all, were adults now, and part of being a grown up is letting go. (Right?) So count to ten quietly to yourself, leave the room, or just close your eyes and take a deep breath. The more you practice these self-calming techniques, the better off youll be when you really need them.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        2.
       </b>
       <b>
        Understand that your role has changedand is still changing.
       </b>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       As your parents age and need more assistance, you start to shift away from your usual role as the child in this relationship. The effect on your psyche can be not only profound but surreal, and is further compounded when a parent strongly resists this shift. (Ive experienced this myself: Mom doesnt want to be treated like a child, yet Im slowly taking charge of the things she and Dad used to handle.)
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       Its a careful balancing act: adopting the decision-making role while not actually assuming the parent role. Be careful not to treat your parents like children. They deserve your respect for their parenthood status, and treating them any differently (even if youre making all the decisions) is a recipe for disaster.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        3.
       </b>
       <b>
        Recognize that you will ALWAYS be the child.
       </b>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       Although Im now 47, when I visit Mom in Indiana she still asks the same questions that annoyed me when I was younger and living at home: What time will you be home? and Can you call me if youll be late? and Why do you choose that particular lipstick?
       <span class="pullquote">
        No matter how old I am or how our roles shift, I will
        <i>
         always
        </i>
        be Moms little girl.
       </span>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       I cant make her stop thinking like my mom, but I
       <i>
        can
       </i>
       change how I react to those questions. The issue here isnt one of her needing to trust me as an adult; rather, its about me letting go of the emotional baggage that defines the parent-child relationship.So instead of being angry when Mom reminds me to be careful (arent I always?), I try to remember to celebrate the fact that she still cares.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        4. Bring along a healthy dose of patience and humor.
       </b>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       The key here is to remember that on a trip your parents are out of their element and might need some extra time (and patience from you!) to handle all these temporary changes in their lives.
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       For example, when I travel with Mom everything needs to
       <i>
        s-s-l-l-o-o-w-w
       </i>
       down. We start our day later, we take more breaks, and we stick to regular meal times (critical to maintaining Moms pill schedule). Ive come to realize that this pace benefits me, too: exploring tourist sites on a more relaxed schedule lets me better appreciate what Im seeing!
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       Ive also noticed that as Mom has aged, shes become less tolerant of cultural differences (a characteristic that makes me a bit anxious when we travel). As someone who lives alone, shes also used to extreme quiet. Throw in my brothers twin 26-month-old girls, toys that constantly make noise (thank you, Thomas the Train), and Baby-TV (the Chinese version of Cartoon Network), and my mom is ready to jump out the window. Making light of these situations helps keep Mom calm and having a good timewhile also preventing me from going over the edge!
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        5.
       </b>
       <b>
        Relax and smell the flowers (and plan activities on your own).
       </b>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       Remember, youre
       <i>
        on vacation
       </i>
       . The goalfor both you and your parentis to relax and get away from your regular routines. Before you start your trip, do some research and identify activities that you and your parent can do together as well as activities you can each do on your own.
       <span class="pullquote">
        Being in contact 24/7 while on vacation can tax even the best of relationships.
       </span>
       So give each other a bit of space and some time alone. Take a walk by yourself, get up early and enjoy a solo breakfast, spend some time in a local gymfind activities that you and your parent can enjoy
       <i>
        separately
       </i>
       . This time apart can help make the time youre together that much more special.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        6.
       </b>
       <b>
        Understand that your parents input matters.
       </b>
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       I treat my solo vacation time as an opportunity to be completely self-absorbed and do only what I want to do (clearly, Im single and have no children). When I travel with other people, I have to shift my expectations to include their input as well. And when I travel with Mom, I have to adjust my outlook even more.
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       When Im traveling with friends, Im fine with them chiming in on where to eat and what we do throughout the day. Interestingly, I dont always give the same respect to Moms input. At times, for example, I feel like shes imposing, and I think, We should just do what
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       want to do. When those feelings arise, I have to remind myself that vacation with Mom is
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       just about me; instead, its something were sharing
       <i>
        together
       </i>
       . I owe it to her to ensure that shes comfortable and happy when were traveling togetherand that Im not suffocating her into submission.
       <i>
        Her input matters
       </i>
       . If it doesnt, then I shouldnt take her on vacation with me at all.
      </p>
      <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
       So when you travel with parents, ask them what
       <i>
        they
       </i>
       want to do and then incorporate their ideas into that days plan. Valuing their contributions is another way to show your love for them.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <p>
       These days, I prefer to
       <a href="http://travelwithagingparents.com/reader-question-much-luggage-bring-trip/" target="_blank">
        travel light
       </a>
       so I can avoid those annoying fees for checked luggage. If airlines could figure out a way to check
       <i>
        my emotional baggage
       </i>
       , though, Id happily pay for that convenience! Until then we all have to learn how to handle our emotional baggage ourselves. Its not as difficult as it seems, though. Just pause, take a deep breath, and remind yourself how lucky you are to be going on a trip with your parent.
      </p>
      <p>
       Now get out there and make those vacation plans!
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
