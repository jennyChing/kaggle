               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 Weve all either sent them or seen them. Kids are growing up online, more publicly than ever before. Embarrassing photos and videos that used to be reserved for family only are now wafting out over the Internet and onto social media networks in galaxies far, far away.
                </p>
               </div>
               <link href="/t/etc/clientlibs/attthread/moduleCommon.css" rel="stylesheet" type="text/css"/>

              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 With a high-fidelity camera and Internet connection in just about every pocket, its easy to get carried away. And lets face it, its beyond amazing that we can share so freely. But the moral question arises, just because we can, should we? As with so many other areas of life, its all about balance, baby.
                </p>

               <div class="article-body">
                <h3>
                 <b>
                  Umm, awkward
                 </b>
                </h3>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 In 2012,
                 <a>
                 </a>
                 <a href="http://parenttechorg.blogspot.com/2012/04/babies-part-of-digital-world-too.html" target="_blank">
                  researchers
                 </a>
                 estimated that more than 80% of children under 2 years old already had a digital profilean online presence of some kindbecause of their parents online activity (ParentTech.Org). But what happens when little Zoe or Adrian arrives at middle school, perhaps a bit gawky in braces and glasses? Will classmates dredge up those adorable photos and use them to
                 <a>
                 </a>
                 <a href="http://www.att.com/t/thread/peaceofmind/education">
                  bully your child
                 </a>
                 ?
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 And once teens are on their own two digital feet, how can we help them not litter their young lives with digital land mines that can blow up their chances at college admissions or job opportunities?
                 <a>
                 </a>
                 <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2011/07/21/technology/social-media-history-becomes-a-new-job-hurdle.html?pagewanted=1&amp;_r=3&amp;adxnnl=1&amp;src=recg&amp;adxnnlx=1388088042-gXvvgtYg8%20xKqFBVTb31sA" target="_blank">
                  The New York Times
                 </a>
                 reports that 75% of employment recruiters are required to do online research on candidates, and 70% admit to rejecting applicants based on information they find online.
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <h3>
                 <b>
                  Theres a happy balance
                 </b>
                </h3>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 Even though kids have been growing up online for about 25 years (impossible, right?), the near-ubiquitous participation in social media has made privacy issues thornier and more difficult for us to sort out. Some parents have taken a firm no-sharing stance, refusing to post pictures of their kids online, anywhere, anytime. Others are massive sharers, even reserving domain names for their unborn children.
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="section inlineimage">
               <div class="article-inline-image-container">
                <div class="article-inline-image-wrapper">

                 <div class="article-inline-image-caption">
                  <p class="article-inline-image-title">
                   Sharing pictures of our kids is great, but its also important to protect their digital legacy.
                  </p>
                  <div class="article-inline-image-description">
                  </div>
                 </div>
                </div>
               </div>
               <link href="/t/etc/clientlibs/attthread/inlineimage.css" rel="stylesheet" type="text/css"/>

              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <p>
                 I believe that theres a happy balance. Our kids need to know how to live online, safely and productively. Its where their futures lie. Here are some tips for establishing young childrens digital legacy and helping them manage it once they take the reins:
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  <b>
                   Think before you share.
                  </b>
                  Could this photo or video come back to haunt your child or your relationship with him? Is this something she will want in her digital portfolio? And even more important for some of us, do your friends
                  <i>
                   really
                  </i>
                  want to see another video of darling Kaitlin scooting across the floor in her diapers? Some things are better stored in your heart and memory instead of on the Internet.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  <b>
                   Share judiciously.
                  </b>
                  You dont have to go all or nothing. There are many ways to share photos online with distant grandparents that dont involve putting them in the public domain. Set up a password-protected site on a photo-sharing site such as
                  <a>
                  </a>
                  <a href="http://www.picasaweb.google.com/" target="_blank">
                   Picasa Web Albums
                  </a>
                  . Apps such as
                  <a>
                  </a>
                  <a href="https://evernote.com/" target="_blank">
                   Evernote
                  </a>
                  let you do much the same thing from your handheld devices.
                  <a>
                  </a>
                  <a href="http://dayoneapp.com/" target="_blank">
                   DayOne
                  </a>
                  is an app for journaling that lets you selectively and easily share entries with others. You can also use Facebook privacy controls and photo albums more than most of us do to limit who sees which photos on that site.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  <b>
                   Be part of your kids online world.
                  </b>
                  As kids begin to venture online themselves, run alongside the bicycle for as long as you need to. Insist on having your kids passwords to their Facebook pages and know whats going on in their online livesjust as you do in their offline lives. Ask them to sign a
                  <a href="http://www.att.com/Common/about_us/files/pdf/mobile_safety/family_media_agreement.pdf" target="_blank">
                   digital citizenship contract
                  </a>
                  that pledges good online behavior. For young ones, make it fun with apps like
                  <a href="https://www.digitalpassport.org/educator-registration" target="_blank">
                   Digital Passport
                  </a>
                  . Check your middle schoolers Facebook page to see if there are postings that might make them feel sad or badand maybe send a quick pick-me-up text. Friend your teens on Facebook; its a valuable glimpse into their world.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  <b>
                   Help teens see their online postings through adult eyes.
                  </b>
                  As your teens wriggle out of your online grasp, help them understand how adults view postings that they and their peers think are like totally hilarious. The college admissions officer or HR director will not be smiling. Show them real examplesyou can probably find them in your own circle of friendsof young people who have had cherished dreams blocked by dumb pictures on Facebook.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="articleParagraph section">
               <div class="article-body">
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  <b>
                   What would grandma say?
                  </b>
                  In his book
                  <i>
                   Public and Permanent: The Golden Rule of the 21
                   <sup>
                    st
                   </sup>
                   Century
                  </i>
                  , author Richard Guerrysuggests that before you do anything with a camera, cell phone, or computer, imagine the person who means the most to you standing over your shoulder. If youre OK with that person seeing what youre about to do, go ahead. If not, dont do it.
                 </li>
                </ul>
               </div>
