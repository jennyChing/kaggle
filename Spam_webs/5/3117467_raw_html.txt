     <p>
      <b>
       Tanegashima
      </b>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">

          ?
         </span>
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </span>
      , also
      <b>
       hinawaj
      </b>
      <span style="font-weight: normal">
       (
       <span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        
       </span>
       <sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets">

        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </span>
      , was a type of
      <a href="/wiki/Matchlock" title="Matchlock">
       matchlock
      </a>
      or
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>

        </span>

       arquebus
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [

       </a>

      <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
       Japan
      </a>
      through the
      <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">
       Portuguese
      </a>
      in 1543.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [

        3

         ]
        </span>

      <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">

      <a href="/wiki/Ashigaru" title="Ashigaru">
       ashigaru

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
       History
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>

       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
       Origins
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanegashima_(Japanese_matchlock)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The tanegashima seems to have been based on
      <a href="/wiki/Snap_matchlock" title="Snap matchlock">
       snap matchlocks
      </a>
      that were produced in
      <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India" title="Portuguese India">
       Portuguese India
      </a>
      , at the armory of
      <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">
       Goa
      </a>
      (a colony of Portugal
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_Conquest_of_Goa_(1510)" title="Portuguese Conquest of Goa (1510)">
       since 1510
      </a>
      ).
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        5
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The name
      <i>
       tanegashima
      </i>
      came from the Japanese island (
      <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima" title="Tanegashima">
       Tanegashima
      </a>
      ) where a Chinese
      <a href="/wiki/Junk_(ship)" title="Junk (ship)">
       junk
      </a>
      with Portuguese adventurers on board was driven to anchor by a storm in 1543. The lord of the Japanese island, Tanegashima Tokitaka (15281579), purchased two matchlock
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muskets" title="Muskets">
       muskets
      </a>
      from the Portuguese and put a swordsmith to work copying the matchlock barrel and firing mechanism. The smith (Yaita) did not have much of a problem with most of the gun but "drilling the barrel helically so that the screw (
      <i>
       bisen
      </i>
      bolt) could be tightly inserted" was a major problem as this "technique did apparently not exist in Japan until this time." The Portuguese fixed their ship and left the island and only in the next year when a Portuguese blacksmith was brought back to Japan was the problem solved.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        6
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Within ten years of its introduction, an upwards of 300,000 tanegashima firearms were reported to have been manufactured.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        7
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Sengoku_period">
       Sengoku period
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanegashima_(Japanese_matchlock)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sengoku period">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ashigaru_using_shields_(tate).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="682" data-file-width="935" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ashigaru_using_shields_%28tate%29.jpg/220px-Ashigaru_using_shields_%28tate%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ashigaru_using_shields_%28tate%29.jpg/330px-Ashigaru_using_shields_%28tate%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ashigaru_using_shields_%28tate%29.jpg/440px-Ashigaru_using_shields_%28tate%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ashigaru_using_shields_(tate).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <i>
         Ashigaru
        </i>
        (foot soldiers) using matchlocks (
        <i>
         tanegashima
        </i>
        ) from behind wooden pavises.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Much of Japan was involved with internecine wars during the
      <a href="/wiki/Sengoku_period" title="Sengoku period">
       Sengoku period
      </a>
      (1467-1603), as feudal lords vied for supremacy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        8
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Matchlock" title="Matchlock">
       Matchlock
      </a>
      guns were introduced about midway through the period, and after their introduction on the battlefield, were used extensively toward the end, and had a decisive role in warfare. In 1549,
      <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">
       Oda Nobunaga
      </a>
      ordered 500 guns to be made for his armies. The benefits of firearms were still relatively questionable, however, compared to other weapons. At the time, guns were still rather primitive and cumbersome. According to one estimate, in 16th century Japan, an archer could fire 15 arrows in the time a gunner would take to load, charge, and shoot a firearm.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrin_p.15_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perrin_p.15-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Effective range also was only 80 to 100 meters, and at that distance a bullet could easily bounce off armour. Matchlocks were vulnerable to humid or rainy conditions as the powder would become damp.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrin_p.15_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perrin_p.15-9">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        9
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      However, one advantage was that a firearm could be manned effectively by farmers or non-
      <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">
       samurai
      </a>
      low-ranking soldiers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrin_p.25_10-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perrin_p.25-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The Japanese soon worked on various techniques to improve the effectiveness of their guns. They developed a serial firing technique to create a continuous rain of bullets on the enemy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrin_p.17_11-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perrin_p.17-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They also developed bigger calibers to increase lethal power.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrin_p.17_11-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perrin_p.17-11">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        11
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Protective boxes in
      <a href="/wiki/Lacquerware" title="Lacquerware">
       lacquerware
      </a>
      were invented to fit over the firing mechanism so it could still fire while it was raining,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        12
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as were systems to accurately fire weapons at night by keeping fixed angles thanks to measured strings.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        13
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="899" data-file-width="2409" height="82" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg/220px-EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg/330px-EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg/440px-EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EdoJapaneseArquebuse.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Various antique tanegashima
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      In 1563 the Amako of Izumo province won a victory over the Kikkawa with 33 of their adversaries wounded by tanegashima,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        14
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      as a result, in the year 1567
      <a href="/wiki/Takeda_Shingen" title="Takeda Shingen">
       Takeda Shingen
      </a>
      announced that, "Hereafter, the guns will be the most important arms, therefore decrease the number of spears per unit, and have your most capable men carry guns".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        15
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">
       Oda Nobunaga
      </a>
      used tanegashima in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anegawa" title="Battle of Anegawa">
       Battle of Anegawa
      </a>
      (1570), and again against the powerful Takeda clan in the
      <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Nagashino" title="Battle of Nagashino">
       Battle of Nagashino
      </a>
      (1575), 3,000 gunners helped win the battle, firing by volleys of a thousand at a time. They were concealed across a river and used breastworks to effectively stop enemy infantry and cavalry charges while being protected.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        16
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      The defeat of the powerful Takeda clan brought about permanent changes in battle tactics.
     </p>
     <p>
      Japan became so enthusiastic about the new weapons that it possibly overtook every European country in absolute numbers produced.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perrin_p.25_10-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Perrin_p.25-10">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        10
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      Japan also used the guns in the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_invasions_of_Korea_(1592%E2%80%931598)" title="Japanese invasions of Korea (15921598)">
       Japanese invasion of Korea
      </a>
      in 1592, in which about a quarter of the invasion force of 160,000 were gunners.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        17
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      They were extremely successful at first and managed to capture
      <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">
       Seoul
      </a>
      just 18 days after their landing at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pusan" title="Pusan">
       Pusan
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        18
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Edo_period">
       Edo period
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanegashima_(Japanese_matchlock)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Edo period">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3330" data-file-width="4100" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg/220px-Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg/330px-Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg/440px-Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bronze_barreled_Japanese_matchlock_pistol.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Antique Japanese (samurai) tanegashima (matchlock) pistol
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The internal war for control of Japan was won by
      <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu" title="Tokugawa Ieyasu">
       Tokugawa Ieyasu
      </a>
      who defeated his rivals at the Battle of Sekigahara in October 1600. Three years later, he established the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tokugawa_Shogunate" title="Tokugawa Shogunate">
       Tokugawa Shogunate
      </a>
      , a powerful entity that would maintain peace, stability, and prosperity in Japan for the following 250 years. This is known as the
      <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">
       Edo period
      </a>
      (1603-1868). From the mid 17th century, Japan decided to close itself to interaction with the West as well as its close neighbors of China and Korea through its policy of
      <a href="/wiki/Sakoku" title="Sakoku">
       Sakoku
      </a>
      . Contrary to popular belief, this did not lead to Japan "giving up the gun." If anything, the gun was used less frequently because the Edo Period did not have many large-scale conflicts in which a gun would be of use. Often the sword was simply the more practical weapon in the average small-scale conflicts. It should also be noted that isolation did not decrease the production of guns in Japanon the contrary, there is evidence of around 200 gunsmiths in Japan by the end of the Edo Period.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        19
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      But the social life of firearms had changed: as the historian David L. Howell has argued, for many in Japanese society, the gun had become less a weapon than a farm implement for scaring off animals.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        20
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      With no external enemies for over 200 years, tanegashima were mainly used by samurai for hunting and target practice, the majority were relegated to the arms store houses of the various feudal lords (
      <a href="/wiki/Daimyo" title="Daimyo">
       daimyo
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1664" data-file-width="2496" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg/220px-Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg/330px-Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg/440px-Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Himeji_Oshiro_Matsuri_August09_007.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Modern tanegashima gun troop in Japan
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The arrival in Japan of the United States Navy led by
      <a href="/wiki/Matthew_C._Perry" title="Matthew C. Perry">
       Commodore Perry
      </a>
      in 1854 started a period of re-armament. The tanegashima was an antiquated weapon by the 1800s and various samurai factions acquired advanced firearms including the
      <a href="/wiki/Mini%C3%A9_rifle" title="Mini rifle">
       mini rifle
      </a>
      , breech-loading and repeating rifles. The samurai era ended in 1868 with the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiji_era" title="Meiji era">
       Meiji era
      </a>
      , the Japanese turned to a national conscription army with modern weapons and uniforms. The last use of samurai armour and traditional weapons including tanegashima in Japan was during the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satsuma_rebellion" title="Satsuma rebellion">
       Satsuma rebellion
      </a>
      (1877), when the Meiji government's newly established
      <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">
       Imperial Japanese Army
      </a>
      put an end to the last samurai and their resistance to modernization.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">
       Modern use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanegashima_(Japanese_matchlock)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Today tanegashima are readily available from sellers of antique firearms and dealers of samurai antiques both in Japan and the West. Modern tanegashima gun troops in Japan re-enact the use of tanegashima in battle and black powder enthusiasts use tanegashima for target practice.
     </p>
