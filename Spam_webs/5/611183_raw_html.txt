          <div id="wsite-content">
           <div class="blog-post" id="blog-post-887997747421571796">
            <div class="blog-header">
             <h2 class="blog-title">
              <a class="blog-title-link blog-link" href="http://www.meangeme.com/home/how-to-get-people-to-like-you-the-psychology-of-attraction">
               How To Get People To Like You: The Psychology of Attraction
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p class="blog-date">
              <span class="date-text">
               8/5/2014
              </span>
             </p>
             <p class="blog-comments">
              <a class="blog-link" href="http://www.meangeme.com/home/how-to-get-people-to-like-you-the-psychology-of-attraction#comments">
               32 Comments
              </a>
             </p>
            </div>
            <div class="blog-separator">
            </div>
            <div class="blog-content">
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              <strong>
               <font size="6">
                <span style="line-height: 24px; background-color: initial;">
                 "Be liked and you will never want."
                </span>
               </font>
               <br/>
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <font size="6">
               - Willy Loman, Death of a Salesman
              </font>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Willy Loman's infatuation with being well liked is something I will never forget about grade 11 English class. There's something about the immense importance he placed on the power of getting people to like you that really resonated with me.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Because as misguided as Willy was, and as tragic as his story was (the title is essentially a spoiler alert), I think he was right.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <strong>
               Typical disclaimer:
              </strong>
              <em>
               It's totally fine if you don't want to purposefully do things to somewhat manipulate people into liking you. This post is for people who are interested in the psychology of attraction, and certain things that could (yes, there are no guarantees) influence people to like you more.
              </em>
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              So how do you get people to like you? How do you become a JLaw?
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/5965468_orig.gif" style="width:100%;max-width:500px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
                Who doesn't love Jennifer Lawrence? No, seriously, name someone who doesn't.
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              1. Self-disclose.
             </h2>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              I've never met a person who didn't love Jennifer Lawrence. I've never actually heard anyone say anything bad about Jennifer Lawrence. She is what I think of when I think of likeability.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              So why the heck does everyone like her so much?
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Because she says pretty much whatever is on her mind. Because
              <strong>
               we feel like we know her.
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              How do you get people to feel like they know you? You self-disclose.
              <br/>
             </div>
             <blockquote style="text-align:left;">
              <font size="5">
               Self disclosure is "a process of communication through which one person reveals himself or herself to another."
              </font>
              <br/>
             </blockquote>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              Self-disclosure doesn't necessarily mean you have to start telling everyone your deepest darkest secrets - sharing your favourite things with someone can also be effective self-disclosure.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <strong>
               Why doesdisclosurework?
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7809308?dopt=Citation" target="_blank" title="">
               Collins and Miller (1994)
              </a>
              found there are three main effects of self-disclosure on liking:
              <br/>
              <span>
              </span>
              <br/>
              <span>
              </span>
              <ul>
               <li>
                People who disclose intimate things about themselves are more liked than people who don't.
               </li>
               <li>
                People disclose more when they like someone.
               </li>
               <li>
                People prefer those they have self-disclosed to.
               </li>
              </ul>
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              2. Don't like
              <em>
               everyone
              </em>
              <em>
               .
              </em>
             </h2>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              In general, people like other people who areselective - AKA, not desperate.
              <br/>
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              <font size="5">
               Eastwick et al., 2007
              </font>
             </h2>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              In 2007, Eastwick et al. conducted seven speed dating sessions for 156 undergrads. At the event, participants had four minute dates with 9 to 13 opposite sex individuals. Immediately afterwards, they completed a two-minute Interaction Record, which included items that assessed how attractive the participant found their speed date - like "
              <em>
               I really liked my partner." and "I was sexually attracted to my partner."
              </em>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              There was also an item that measured
              <strong>
               perceived unselectivity
              </strong>
              - "
              <em>
               To what percentage of the other people here today will this person say 'yes'
              </em>
              ?"
              <br/>
              <br/>
              When they got home, participants used a website to record whether or not they would be interested in seeing the people they speed dated again. A la
              <a href="http://www.gotinder.com/" target="_blank" title="">
               Tinder
              </a>
              , if both participants said yes, they were given the opportunity to contact each other.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              The results were analyzed, and they found that:
              <br/>
              <br/>
              1. If a participant was
              <strong>
               uniquely
              </strong>
              interested ina specific speed dater, the speed dater was likely to reciprocate that unique desire.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              2. A participant's
              <strong>
               unique romantic desire positively predicted
              </strong>
              their speed date partner's experience of unique chemistry with the participant.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              3. If a participant basically liked everyone they met, everyone tended not to like them back. Also, if a participant liked everyone, people were
              <strong>
               less likely
              </strong>
              to report chemistry with that participant.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              So though it may seem like you're increasing your odds of getting into relationship by liking everyone, it can come off as desperate, which, in fact,
              <strong>
               decreases
              </strong>
              your chance of finding a romantic match.
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/6407112_orig.gif" style="width:100%;max-width:420px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
                Probably why this strategy isn't the most effective.
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              3. Do exciting things with the people you want to like you.
             </h2>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              <font size="5">
               Dutton &amp; Aron, 1974
              </font>
              <br/>
              <span>
              </span>
             </h2>
             <div class="paragraph">
              Back in 1974, Dutton &amp; Aron placed one of their attractive research assistants, 'Gloria', on two different bridges at two different times.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              One of these bridges was a control bridge - it was
              <strong>
               sturdy.
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              The other one of these bridges was suspended, and very
              <strong>
               shaky
              </strong>
              .
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-thin " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a href="http://pixdaus.com/shaky-bridge-bridge/items/view/30964/" target="_blank">
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/9971478_orig.jpg" style="width:100%;max-width:400px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
                Source: Pixadus
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              Gloria stopped a bunch of men on each bridge and asked them to make up a story about this photo(known as a
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thematic_Apperception_Test" target="_blank" title="">
               Thematic Apperception Test
              </a>
              ):
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/5956903.png?421" style="width:auto;max-width:100%"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              Gloria recorded the men's responses on both bridges, which were rated for sexual imagery afterwards, using this scale:
              <br/>
              <br/>
              5: sexual intercourse
              <br/>
              4: lover
              <br/>
              3: kiss
              <br/>
              2: girlfriend
              <br/>
              1: no sexual content
              <br/>
              <br/>
              On the
              <strong>
               sturdy bridge
              </strong>
              , responses were, on average, rated
              <strong>
               1.41
              </strong>
              .
              <br/>
              On the
              <strong>
               shaky bridge
              </strong>
              , responses were, on average, rated
              <strong>
               2.57
              </strong>
              .
              <br/>
              <br/>
              In a kind of fun twist, Gloria also gave her number to participants, which they could call if they had any "questions."
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Of the participants who were on the
              <strong>
               sturdy
              </strong>
              bridge,
              <strong>
               only 13% called Gloria after the experiment
              </strong>
              .
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Of the participants who were on the
              <strong>
               shaky
              </strong>
              bridge,
              <strong>
               50% called Gloria after the
              </strong>
              <strong>
               experiment
              </strong>
              .
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Why did this happen?
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Dutton and Aron chalked it up to
              <strong>
               <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misattribution_of_arousal" target="_blank" title="">
                misattribution of arousal
               </a>
              </strong>
              - the process whereby people make a mistake in assuming what's actually making them feel aroused.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <strong>
               Note:
              </strong>
              Aroused is often used in psych to describe the physiological and psychological state of being awake or reactive to a stimuli (so we're not just talking about sexual arousal, just general arousal).
              <br/>
              <br/>
              On the shaky bridge, participants were likely feeling more physically aroused than usual - probably because the bridge was high up and felt like it could break at any time.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              What Dutton and Aron proposed was that participants took these feelings of physical arousal - like a faster heartbeat, or sweaty palms - and
              <strong>
               instead of realizing that they were feeling this way because of the bridge, participants misattributed these physical symptoms as a reaction to the photo, or as attraction to Gloria.
              </strong>
              <br/>
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-thin " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a href="http://www.dailymail.co.uk/femail/article-2270218/Valentines-Day-2013-ideas-Take-plunge-lover-tandem-bungee-jump.html" target="_blank">
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/7744993.jpg?482" style="width:auto;max-width:100%"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
                Source: Daily Mail
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              But, this kind of goes both ways - you can take your love interest bungee jumping and hope that a little bit of misattribution happens, and that they start to like you more - but, you also have to realize that you could be misattributing your arousal as feelings for them.
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              4. Show people that you like them.
             </h2>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/2200583_orig.gif" style="width:100%;max-width:500px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              This one's basically the opposite of the Grade 2 call him names thing - you laugh, but I did that for years, and it's a really, really hard habit to kick. Even as an adult, I find myself wanting to be aloof, or wanting to play "hard to get" when I actually like someone.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              But more often than not, this makes people think that you actually don't like them, which can have negative effects on your end game:
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              <font size="5">
               Curtis &amp; Miller (1986)
              </font>
              <br/>
              <span>
              </span>
             </h2>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              Curtis and Miller told participants that someone either liked them or didn't like them after the two participants had a 10 minute conversation with each other. They found that a participant's liking of the other person was dramatically affected:
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/4296696_orig.png" style="width:100%;max-width:563px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              As you can see, when Person A thought that Person B liked them, they liked them more than when they were told that Person B disliked them.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              This study exemplifies why it's okay to act like you like someone. Because if you're always playing hard to get, it could seem like you don't like someone - which, in turn, will make them like you less.
             </div>
             <h2 class="wsite-content-title" style="text-align:left;">
              5. Ask for favours.
             </h2>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              This is a way to take advantage of the
              <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Franklin_effect" target="_blank" title="">
               Ben Franklin effect
              </a>
              (and I do this a lot more than I'd like to admit).
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Essentially, if someone does a favour for you, they're more likely to do you another favour for you than they would be if you had done them a favour in the first place.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              That's a lot of words, so I'll give an example. If I ask Sarah to get me a drink, and she does, she'll be more likely to get me another drink in the future - even more so than she would be if I had gotten her a drink instead.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <strong>
               Why does this lead to liking?
              </strong>
              <br/>
              <br/>
              A theory that explains why getting people to do favours for you can make them like you more involves
              <strong>
               <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_dissonance" target="_blank">
                cognitive dissonance
               </a>
              </strong>
              - that people change their attitudes or behaviour to resolve the tension between their thoughts, attitudes, and actions.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              In this case, if the person who did you a favour had any negative feelings toward you, they would replace these feelings with more positive ones - because they did you a favour, and that means that they like you... right?
             </div>
             <div>
              <div class="wsite-image wsite-image-border-none " style="padding-top:10px;padding-bottom:10px;margin-left:0;margin-right:0;text-align:center">
               <a>
                <img alt="Picture" src="/uploads/1/0/6/1/10616136/1397294_orig.gif" style="width:100%;max-width:485px"/>
               </a>
               <div style="display:block;font-size:90%">
               </div>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="paragraph" style="text-align:left;">
              Unsurprisingly, there are a lot of things that go into getting people to like you.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Though it was easy to pick the top five factors that I found interesting, it's a little harder to actually use this knowledge. Nevertheless, I hope you learned something new that you think you might be able to apply to your life.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              If this helps you get your future husband or wife, let me know.
