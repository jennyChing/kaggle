     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Long Mynd
      </b>
      is a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heathland" title="Heathland">
       heath
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">
       moorland
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">
       plateau
      </a>
      that forms part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Shropshire_Hills" title="Shropshire Hills">
       Shropshire Hills
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">
       Shropshire
      </a>
      , England. The high ground, which is designated as an
      <a href="/wiki/Area_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" title="Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty">
       Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty
      </a>
      , lies between the
      <a href="/wiki/Stiperstones" title="Stiperstones">
       Stiperstones
      </a>
      range to the west and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stretton_Hills" title="Stretton Hills">
       Stretton Hills
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Wenlock_Edge" title="Wenlock Edge">
       Wenlock Edge
      </a>
      to the east. Much of it is owned and managed by the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">
       National Trust
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The Long Mynd, which is approximately 7 miles (11km) long by a maximum of 3 miles (4.8km) wide, has steep valleys on its eastern flanks while its western side is marked by a slope that rises in a steep escarpment from the wide valley of the River East Onny. It is surrounded by the principal settlements of
      <a href="/wiki/Church_Stretton" title="Church Stretton">
       Church Stretton
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Little_Stretton,_Shropshire" title="Little Stretton, Shropshire">
       Little Stretton
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/All_Stretton" title="All Stretton">
       All Stretton
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulverbatch" title="Pulverbatch">
       Pulverbatch
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Smethcott" title="Smethcott">
       Smethcott
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Woolstaston" title="Woolstaston">
       Woolstaston
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Asterton" title="Asterton">
       Asterton
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Myndtown" title="Myndtown">
       Myndtown
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Wentnor" title="Wentnor">
       Wentnor
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ratlinghope" title="Ratlinghope">
       Ratlinghope
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      The two highest points on the Long Mynd, Pole Bank (516m (1,693ft)) and Caer Caradoc (459m (1,506ft)), are both classed as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marilyn_(hill)" title="Marilyn (hill)">
       Marilyns
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
       Etymology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The name
      <i>
       Long Mynd
      </i>
      means "Long Mountain". In
      <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
       Welsh
      </a>
      it is named the
      <i>
       Mynydd Hir
      </i>
      <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
       <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Welsh" title="Help:IPA for Welsh">
        [mn hir]
       </a>
      </span>
      .
     </p>

     <p>
      The geology dates back to
      <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">
       Precambrian
      </a>
      , and during the time would have been 60 south of the
      <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
       equator
      </a>
      , the same latitude as the
      <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">
       Falkland Islands
      </a>
      . Shropshire would have been at the very edge of a large
      <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
       continent
      </a>
      near the sea, which was being buckled by tectonic activity, causing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanoes" title="Volcanoes">
       volcanoes
      </a>
      to form. The area had broad rivers; evidence of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudflats" title="Mudflats">
       mudflats
      </a>
      has been found. The rivers would have flowed out to sea, creating large
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Estuaries" title="Estuaries">
       estuaries
      </a>
      ; over time, the mudflats would have built up, and volcanic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eruption" title="Eruption">
       eruptions
      </a>
      deposited ash in layers between the sand and mud. The primary rock of the Long Mynd is sandstone, usually coloured purple or grey. The volcanoes created the nearby
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stretton_Hills" title="Stretton Hills">
       Stretton Hills
      </a>
      and the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wrekin" title="Wrekin">
       Wrekin
      </a>
      , and eruptions would have been frequent. There are layers in the rocks of the Long Mynd that have preserved raindrop marks recording a passing rain shower. The raindrop marks were created when rain fell onto a layer of firm dry mud, and were then covered by another layer of mud, which filled them in and preserved them for 565 million years. Examples of these
      <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
       fossilised
      </a>
      rain prints can be viewed today in the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">
       National Trust
      </a>
      Tearoom Exhibition, in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carding_Mill_Valley" title="Carding Mill Valley">
       Carding Mill Valley
      </a>
      . The layers of rock built up over the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennia" title="Millennia">
       millennia
      </a>
      to create an approximately 7,000m (23,000ft) thick layer composed of sand, mud, silt and ash.
     </p>
     <p>
      Towards the end of the Precambrian period, the volcanoes ceased their eruptions, and the rivers had dried up. Instead the forces that created the volcanoes caused the new rocks to lift and fold, creating mountains and valleys in the area. Much of the rock was melted during this period, underneath the Earth's
      <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
       crust
      </a>
      , causing the mountains to continually change towards the latter part of the Precambrian. The Church Stretton Fault line was created during this period, and is still active today. The hill Caer Caradoc adjacent to the Long Mynd and from the same time is more volcanic in origin, and is thought to be the remnants of the great mountain chain.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">
       Cambrian
      </a>
      , Shropshire was flooded by the sea, after the Global Ice Age ended 545 million years ago. Thick layers of beach pebbles and white sand were built up against the sea cliffs that were once molten lava. During this time, the shallow sea played host to the huge explosion of new life which occurred during the Cambrian. Shropshire has some of the most historically important evidence in the explosion of life and in the naming and dividing of the Cambrian period.
      <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">
       Trilobites
      </a>
      that are found in the county are internationally important for deciding how the Cambrian is divided into smaller segments of time.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">
       Ordovician
      </a>
      had Shropshire back to volcanic activity, and saw the county temporarily split in two, along the Pontesford  Linley fault line. Everything west of this line was ocean, while the east was dry land. The
      <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">
       Iapetus Ocean
      </a>
      was closing, bringing the two-halves of Britain towards each other, and volcanic eruptions created the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumbrian_Mountains" title="Cumbrian Mountains">
       Cumbrian Mountains
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia" title="Snowdonia">
       Snowdonia
      </a>
      . Shropshire also saw volcanic activity. To the west of the Pontesford  Linley fault, volcanic rocks have been found. The other side of the fault line was quieter. The land was slowly eroded, and the sea gradually flooded it, so that only the tops of hills could be seen, such as the ancient Caer Caradoc. Towards the end of the Ordovician, the sea levels dropped, due to another ice age.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">
       Silurian
      </a>
      period, occurring 439 million years ago, has been well preserved nearby, in
      <a href="/wiki/Wenlock_Edge" title="Wenlock Edge">
       Wenlock Edge
      </a>
      . During this time, Shropshire would have been flooded again by shallow sea. Wenlock Edge would have formed during this time, and the fossils of ancient
      <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">
       corals
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">
       shellfish
      </a>
      can be found all along the Edge, preserved in
      <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
       limestone
      </a>
      . Towards the end of the Silurian the Iapetus would have fully closed, and England and
      <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
       Scotland
      </a>
      were joined. The closing of this ocean was important to geology in
      <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
       Great Britain
      </a>
      . It caused most of our hills and mountains to align along the fault, northeast to the southwest.
     </p>
     <p>
      During the
      <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">
       Devonian
      </a>
      , the newly formed Scottish mountains had rivers flowing all over the land. Shropshire was no exception; these new rivers caused thick deposits in the area. Most of the rocks from this era are red sandstones, caused by iron in the rock. The area was known as the Old Red Sandstone Continent. These river sediments have traces of fossilised fish. Shropshire would have remained above water until the end of the Devonian, when the seas rose once again.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">
       Carboniferous
      </a>
      was a time of great change for the area. Shropshire would have been near the equator, and the Old Red Sandstone continent had been eroded away; in the early part of the era, the county was under a shallow sea. However, tectonic activity pushed Britain out of the sea. South of Shropshire this effect was felt greatly, though Shropshire was relatively quiet. Mountains to the north were being worn down by rivers, creating enormous deltas that were colonised by plant life. A tropical forest took hold all over Shropshire, with ancient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ferns" title="Tree ferns">
       tree ferns
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horsetails" title="Horsetails">
       horsetails
      </a>
      . Shropshire eventually crossed the equator during this era, and became a part of
      <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">
       Pangaea
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">
       Permian
      </a>
      ; the area would have been very similar to the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">
       Sahara Desert
      </a>
      , and would have been in the vicinity, around 20 to 30 north of the equator.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">
       Triassic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">
       Jurassic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">
       Cretaceous
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">
       Tertiary
      </a>
      were very quiet in Shropshire, and very little evidence can be found from these periods. The last
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">
       Ice Age
      </a>
      during the
      <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">
       Quaternary
      </a>
      has its effect on all of Shropshire, shaping the landscape as we see it today. The Long Mynd would have been under a thick Ice Sheet, several hundred metres thick. As the ice melted, it carved out the valleys and hills of the Long Mynd massif we see today. The small rivers, streams and brooks still very slowly carve out the valleys. The springs and bogs play a part in Church Stretton's economy, as the people bottle the mineral water that comes from the Long Mynd.
     </p>
     <p>
      Today the steep and narrow valleys are covered in a thin layer of soil, with a low
      <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">
       pH
      </a>
      , able to support only strong
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">
       grasses
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Juncus" title="Juncus">
       rushes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">
       heathers
      </a>
      . Beneath the soil the evidence of the ancient and chequered past can be seen, and the rocky
      <a href="/wiki/Outcrop" title="Outcrop">
       outcrops
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Scree" title="Scree">
       scree
      </a>
      slopes are excellent places to view the different layers of ancient rock.
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 2006,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">
       Cambridge University
      </a>
      has monitored
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic" title="Seismic">
       seismic
      </a>
      activity in Long Mynd. The broadband
      <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">
       seismometer
      </a>
      is connected to the internet, and real-time traces can be viewed online
      <a class="external text" href="http://bullard.esc.cam.ac.uk/~mwd27/seismometers.html#hlmb" rel="nofollow">
       here
      </a>
      .
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Bronze_Age">
       Bronze Age
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bronze Age">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">
       Bronze Age
      </a>
      period is by far the most recorded period of time on the Long Mynd. There are dozens of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tumuli" title="Tumuli">
       tumuli
      </a>
      on the moorland. Some are small, the remains of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamber_tombs" title="Chamber tombs">
       chamber tombs
      </a>
      for example. Others are quite large, the sites on the Long Mynd, from the Bronze Age, include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" title="Dike (construction)">
       Dykes
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">
       Barrows
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Barristers Plain
       <a href="/wiki/Cross_dyke" title="Cross dyke">
        Cross-Ridge Dyke
       </a>
      </b>
      runs south-west to north-east, almost in a straight line for 170m (560ft). It runs across the narrowest area of a
      <a href="/wiki/Ridge" title="Ridge">
       ridge
      </a>
      between Grindle Hill and Round Hill. The remains of the dyke is now covered in heather, and is approximately 5.5m (18ft), and is 0.5m (1.6ft). On its western side it is fronted by a 3m (9.8ft) wide
      <a href="/wiki/Ditch" title="Ditch">
       ditch
      </a>
      . At both ends of the Cross Ridge Dyke, it fades into the steep hillside. A gap in the dyke, 60m (200ft) from the south-east end, is thought to make room for a trackway along the ridge. Its purpose was to cut off Grindle Hill from the main
      <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">
       plateau
      </a>
      , and to create a barrier for access from the west.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Devil's Mouth Cross-Ridge Dyke
      </b>
      lies between Cardingmill Valley and Townbrook Valley. The dyke is 140m (460ft) long, but is cut though by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burway" title="Burway">
       Burway
      </a>
      road and a small car park, 35m (115ft) of the dyke is now missing. Both ends of the dyke end on steep slopes! It was built with stone and earth, and is 6m (20ft) wide, and 1.5m (4.9ft) high, with shallow ditches either side. It is roughly 1,500 years old. It was probably built to control the access along the ancient east to west route, which still crosses the Long Mynd today by means of a modern road.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       High Park Cross-Ridge Dyke
      </b>
      , another dyke on the Long Mynd. At 380m (1,250ft) it is the longest on the Long Mynd. It is approximately 6m (20ft) wide, but in areas survives only as a crop mark. The highest point of the dyke, on the west side of the bank, stands at 1.2m (3.9ft) high, and reaches its widest point at 8m (26ft). A trackway, like that found on Barristers Plain Cross-Ridge Dyke, cuts through the dyke.
     </p>
     <p>
      Of the
      <b>
       Long Mynd Barrows
      </b>
      , over twenty scatter the plateau. The best examples are in the northern area of the Long Mynd. Robin Hoods Butts' barrow, near Duckley Nap, are two well known barrows, and the largest on the Long Mynd, being approximately 36m (118ft) in diameter and 4m (13ft) high.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Shooting Box Barrow
      </b>
      is named after a grouse-shooting hut that stood on the site until it was removed in 1992. It is the only known example of a disc barrow in Shropshire. 21m (68ft) in diameter and 2.3m (7.5ft) high, it is in the centre of a flat circular enclosure 54m (177ft) in diameter, the edge of which is defined by a 5m (16ft) wide bank, which has been partially destroyed by a modern path. It had been dated between c.1950-1700
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Christ" title="Before Christ">
       BC
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       The Port Way
      </b>
      is an ancient trackway, which runs the length of the Long Mynd massif, and is the largest historical feature on the Long Mynd, at just over 5 miles (8.0km) long. It is still walked today, and is part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Shropshire_Way" title="Shropshire Way">
       Shropshire Way
      </a>
      , and a road that goes to the Gliding Club. A common misconception is that it goes over Pole Bank, but instead it bypasses the hill, following its contours.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age">
       Iron Age
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Iron Age">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Very little
      <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">
       Iron Age
      </a>
      human activity has been recorded on the Long Mynd. However, this later period has a specific ancient site.
     </p>
     <p>
      <b>
       Bodbury Ring Hill Fort
      </b>
      , a
      <a href="/wiki/Hill_fort" title="Hill fort">
       hill fort
      </a>
      above Cardingmill Valley, and sits on the top of Bodbury Hill at 380m (1,250ft). This feature dates from the Iron Age, c. 500BC, and is therefore much later than other ancient sites on the Long Mynd. Another hill fort nearby sits on the summit of
      <a href="/wiki/Caer_Caradoc" title="Caer Caradoc">
       Caer Caradoc
      </a>
      . Bodbury Ring is now looked after by
      <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">
       The National Trust
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">
       18th century
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 18th century">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      During the 18th century,
      <a href="/wiki/Church_Stretton" title="Church Stretton">
       Church Stretton
      </a>
      began to grow in the wide valley between the Long Mynd and Caer Caradoc, as a
      <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">
       market town
      </a>
      , and later a spa. Historically the town was known for its textiles, specifically in Cardingmill Valley. Carding Mill was built in the 18th century, and named after a stage in making cloth, the three stages being
      <a href="/wiki/Carding" title="Carding">
       carding
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Spinning_(textiles)" title="Spinning (textiles)">
       spinning
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">
       weaving
      </a>
      . Carding would have been done by children, and involved using a hand-card that removed and untangled short fibres from the mass of raw material. The cards were wooden blocks with handles and covered in metal spikes, which were angled, (to make it easier to untangle) and set in leather. When untangled, the material would be spun, and then weaved into the final product. The mill is still in the valley today, but has been converted into luxury apartments. The Long Mynd Hotel in Church Stretton was built in 1900, originally as the Hydro, at a time when the town was popular as a spa.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">
       20th century
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 20th century">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A large area of the Long Mynd (almost all its upland area) was bought by the
      <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">
       National Trust
      </a>
      in 1965, and was designated an
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AONB" title="AONB">
       AONB
      </a>
      as part of the
      <a href="/wiki/Shropshire_Hills_AONB" title="Shropshire Hills AONB">
       Shropshire Hills
      </a>
      in 1958.
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Valleys_Hollows_and_Batches">
       Valleys Hollows and Batches
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

     <p>
      There are dozens of
      <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">
       valleys
      </a>

      </i>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dale_(origin)" title="Dale (origin)">
        dales
       </a>

        brooks
       </a>
      </i>
      to distinguish them from the larger features. The main valleys are listed below in alphabetical order.

         </a>
        </div>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Schleicher_ASK_21" title="Schleicher ASK 21">
         Schleicher ASK 21
        </a>
        glider being bungee launched from the Long Mynd
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The area is popular with tourists, and there are many fine walks in the area. Two major walks are the
      <a href="/wiki/Shropshire_Way" title="Shropshire Way">
       Shropshire Way
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="/wiki/Jack_Mytton_Way" title="Jack Mytton Way">
       Jack Mytton Way
      </a>
      . The Long Mynd is also used for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse_riding" title="Horse riding">
       horse riding
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">
       mountain biking
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        1
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      on the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bridleway" title="Bridleway">
       bridleways
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Of the many valleys on the Long Mynd, the most popular is Cardingmill Valley, which has a road directly connected to Church Stretton, and is the National Trust's centre for the area.
     </p>
     <p>
      The windward slopes are popular with
      <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">
       glider
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hang-glider" title="Hang-glider">
       hang-glider
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraglider" title="Paraglider">
       paraglider
      </a>
      pilots.
     </p>
     <p>
      During wintry conditions, some valleys are used as makeshift
      <a href="/wiki/Ski" title="Ski">
       ski
      </a>
      slopes. While highly attractive in good conditions, the Long Mynd can be treacherous in severe weather, and has claimed many lives. In 2015 "snow gates" were installed at various points of access onto the Long Mynd, to deter motorists planning to use the routes in wintry conditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        2
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        <span>
         [
        </span>
        3
        <span>
         ]
        </span>
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Longmynd_hike&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Longmynd hike (page does not exist)">
       Longmynd hike
      </a>
      is a 50-mile (80km) competitive hike that must be completed in under 24 hours. It takes place annually, usually in the first weekend in October. It crosses over the Long Mynd twice, taking in Pole Bank along its course. The hike has been running since 1967.
     </p>
     <p>
      There is a
      <a href="/wiki/Golf_course" title="Golf course">
       golf course
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_Stretton_Golf_Club" title="Church Stretton Golf Club">
       Church Stretton Golf Club
      </a>
      , situated near the Cardingmill Valley, on slopes of Stanyeld Hill and Bodbury Hill. The
      <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clubhouse" title="wikt:clubhouse">
       clubhouse
      </a>
      is at approximately 230 metres (750ft)
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" title="Above sea level">
       above sea level
      </a>
      and the hilly
      <a href="/wiki/Links_(golf)" title="Links (golf)">
       links
      </a>
      course rises up to around 375m (1,230ft). It is the oldest 18-hole golf course in Shropshire, opened in 1898, and one of the highest in the country.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gliding">
       Gliding
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gliding">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The Long Mynd has been home to the Midland Gliding Club since 1934. The club owns 136ha of land on the south end and flies gliders there throughout the year. It runs residential training courses and offers trial lesson flights to members of the public. Many long flights have started from the Long Mynd, most recently one of 750km during the summer of 2007. Midland Gliding Club is one of the last clubs in Europe to regularly launch gliders by
      <a href="/wiki/Gliding#Bungee_launch" title="Gliding">
       bungee
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">
       Flora and fauna
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The typical plant life on the Long Mynd is heather, rushes, and grasses. The wildlife includes ponies, sheep,
      <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">
       bats
      </a>
      and wild birds including the rare
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring_Ouzel" title="Ring Ouzel">
       Ring Ouzel
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">
       Popular culture
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Mynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Popular culture">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The Revd E. D. Carr's
      <i>
       A Night in the Snow
      </i>
      describes his experience, in 1865, of surviving a winter's night on the Long Mynd when attempting to walk home after conducting a Sunday service and visiting an isolated parishioner. He spent 23 hours struggling to force a route back to civilisation.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Long Mynd features in literature in the poetry of
      <a href="/wiki/A._E._Housman" title="A. E. Housman">
       A. E. Housman
      </a>
      , the novels of
      <a href="/wiki/Mary_Webb" title="Mary Webb">
       Mary Webb
      </a>
      (in particular
      <i>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gone_to_Earth_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gone to Earth (novel) (page does not exist)">
        Gone To Earth
       </a>
      </i>
      ), and
      <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Saville" title="Malcolm Saville">
       Malcolm Saville
      </a>
      's Lone Pine series for children.
     </p>
