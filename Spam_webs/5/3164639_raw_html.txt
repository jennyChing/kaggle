  <meta content="Hey there - were Hipmob. We help you communicate with your mobile customers and provide amazing marketing and customer support. You can learn more here.

 The early trends in mobile commerce (iOS and Android specifically) point to a simple but startling conclusion; iOS is for selling product, and Android is for advertising. While this conclusion isnt exhaustive, it mirrors the way Google manages Android, and Apple manages iOS. It also gives businesses and developers a blueprint for allocating your resources to each platform.

 The purchase and usage behavior of consumers on each operating system drive this conclusion; iOS users buy more stuff, use more apps and browse more webpages. Android users simply, dont. At a device level, consumers are increasingly replacing PCs with iPads - this story isnt (yet) mirrored for Android tablets. Android (famously) has far more users. We see this every day at Hipmob.

 User Engagement (measured by messages exchanged): iOS Dominates Even Though Android Has More Devices (Source: Hipmob Research)

 This means different things for different businesses.

 Android Is A Billboard: Place Ads And Get Reach

 If online and mobile sales are a marginal part of your business, but you spend a lot on advertising, then you cant ignore Android. Take consumer packaged goods for example (particularly soft drinks, snacks, toiletries etc), where the cost of shipping, premium prices and the wait for delivery can all dis-incentivize online shoppers. (Emarketer) Most CPG brands still spend massive amounts on advertising, and touch a lot of peoples lives (the Coca Cola brand, according to one study, is recognized by 94% of the worlds population, according to one study).

 For brands like these, the goal is to be top of mind when a consumer walks into Target, Safeway or their local convenience store. CPG products are consumed by every demographic, regardless of background, nationality or income level, so any demographic differences (real or perceived) between Android and iOS hardly matter.

 The need to be top of mind drives massive ad spend - Coca Cola and Pepsico spent over $200m combined on Super Bowl Ads in the last 10 years.. The Super Bowl in 2013 reached approximately 108 million viewers. Compared to this, a platform like Android with a billion activations is like having a billboard in a billion peoples hands. How much would it cost to preload a Coca Cola app (or sponsor preloaded apps) across a ton of future Android devices? Probably less than 10 years worth of SuperBowl spots. Android developers take note.

 **E-Commerce On Android Pales In Comparison To iOS (*Source: Hipmob Research)*


 In this world, if your brand has no reasonable expectation that users will buy your product online (careful here - your space might just not be disrupted YET), Android is an unprecedented advertising opportunity. This makes even more sense if you consider that Google has architected Android to maximize reach. The OS is free, and any OEM can customize and release Android based devices. Why is it free? Because if more people have internet access, theyll search for more stuff, and Google, as the default search service, stands to benefit. Google can gather more data and sell more ads against those searches. This advantage is even more durable if you not only control the search service, but also the OS in which it lives. If Googles primary goal with Android is to sell more ads, why shouldnt yours be? Thats what its made for!

 iOS Is Your Store: Shoppers Come There To Buy

 For ecommerce businesses, iOS is already a force. In 6 short years, categories as varied as hotels, apparel, alcohol, and family goods have transitioned nearly half their revenues to mobile. Most of these sales come from iPhone or iPad users.

 Companies like HauteLook, Wine.com, and Quidsi (the retailer behind Diapers.com) show that its quite plausible to build a true commerce model on top of iOS, in a way that has yet to be true for Android. This is true for apps and the mobile web alike. Retailers like Tea Collection and Wine.com are seeing massive growth in mobile web sales, while folks like HauteLook and Gilt are seeing massive growth in mobile app sales. Most of this growth is coming from iOS - Ive yet to encounter a single e-commerce case where Android makes up more than 15% of mobile sales.

 In contrast to Android which monetizes with ads, the iOS and iPhone/iPad ecosystem profits (quite heartily) from from its users - they buy devices, accessories, and apps. On the iOS App Store, you cant even download a free app without verifying your credit card! Thats right; in order to download a free app, you must first verify your payment credentials with Apple. Nuts.

 

 iOS has been built with massive trust in mind; Apple users buy more digital goods, buy more offline goods, Apple has every users credit card, and the nuances of the experiences built on iOS already support pure commerce. In addition, the empirical evidence points that way - at Hipmob weve talked to hundreds of brands and retailers, and interviewed several; every single one generates substantially more revenue from iOS than from Android.

 Ecommerce Revenues On Android Pale Vs. iOS (Source: Hipmob Research)


 &ldquo;33% of our traffic comes from iOS and 5% comes from Android.&rdquo;
- Sarah Knup, Head of Marketing &amp; Strategy, Tea Collection

 Apple, with iOS,  has assembled a demographic that likes to buy online, and buys in growing numbers. Android has assembled 3X as many activations as iOS. So what you do depends on your business. If your business depends on consumer advertising coupled with offline sales, then you should  weight your Android strategy towards getting more eyeballs and making your brand ubiquitous, whether that means an developing or sponsoring apps, mobile sites or landing pages, mobile ads, or anything else. This doesnt preclude advertising or brand/content marketing on iOS - with ~500m devices activated, it cant be ignored. This just means that if your goal is purely reach, Android gets you more  bang for your buck. If online sales are a large or fast growing part of your business then youll probably have to invest much more heavily in iOS. Your competitors already are, and theyre seeing real results.

      <h3>
       <span>
        iOS Is For Revenue, Android Is For Ads
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="text">
       <p>
        Hey there - were
        <a href="http://www.hipmob.com">
         Hipmob
        </a>
        . We help you communicate with your mobile customers and provide amazing marketing and customer support.
        <a href="http://www.hipmob.com">
         You can learn more here.
        </a>
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <p>
        The early trends in mobile commerce (iOS and Android specifically) point to a simple but startling conclusion;
        <strong>
         iOS is for selling product, and Android is for advertising
        </strong>
        . While this conclusion isnt exhaustive, it mirrors the way Google manages Android, and Apple manages iOS. It also gives businesses and developers a blueprint for allocating your resources to each platform.
       </p>
       <p>
        The purchase and usage behavior of consumers on each operating system drive this conclusion; iOS users buy more stuff, use more apps and browse more webpages. Android users simply, dont. At a device level, consumers are increasingly replacing PCs with iPads - this story isnt (yet) mirrored for Android tablets. Android (famously) has far more users. We see this every day at
        <a href="http://www.hipmob.com">
         Hipmob.
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         User Engagement (measured by messages exchanged): iOS Dominates Even Though Android Has More Devices (
         <a href="http://bit.ly/hipmoblibrary">
          <em>
           Source: Hipmob Research
          </em>
         </a>
         )
        </strong>
        <img alt="" src="https://draftin.com:443/images/1508?token=RYbuKvUL5SuzJwv0S3cNkwVQrpZ_y8ii-USavtPsduQRU1p4tThhq-cJggmjgbehRE8pQoCcsm4BNrSCnG9fyQY"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        This means different things for different businesses.
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Android Is A Billboard: Place Ads And Get Reach
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        If online and mobile sales are a marginal part of your business, but you spend a lot on advertising, then you cant ignore Android. Take consumer packaged goods for example (particularly soft drinks, snacks, toiletries etc), where the cost of shipping, premium prices and the wait for delivery can all dis-incentivize online shoppers. (
        <a href="http://www.emarketer.com/Article/CPG-Brands-Bridge-Gap-Between-Ecommerce-Shoppers-Buyers/1009741#lObC7xVrKGzBfR7g.99">
         Emarketer
        </a>
        ) Most CPG brands still spend massive amounts on advertising, and touch a lot of peoples lives (the Coca Cola brand,
        <a href="http://steelmediainc.com/case-studies/22/coke/">
         according to one study, is recognized by 94% of the worlds population, according to one study
        </a>
        ).
       </p>
       <p>
        For brands like these, the goal is to be top of mind when a consumer walks into Target, Safeway or their local convenience store. CPG products are consumed by every demographic, regardless of background, nationality or income level, so any demographic differences (real or perceived) between Android and iOS hardly matter.
       </p>
       <p>
        The need to be top of mind drives massive ad spend -
        <a href="http://wire.kapitall.com/investment-idea/game-on-here-are-the-top-superbowl-ad-spenders-in-the-last-decade/">
         Coca Cola and Pepsico spent over $200m combined on Super Bowl Ads in the last 10 years.
        </a>
        . The Super Bowl in 2013 reached
        <a href="http://www.ibtimes.com/super-bowl-2013-how-many-people-watched-baltimore-ravens-over-san-francisco-49ers-1060698">
         approximately 108 million viewers
        </a>
        . Compared to this, a platform like Android with a billion activations is like having a billboard in a billion peoples hands. How much would it cost to preload a Coca Cola app (or sponsor preloaded apps) across a ton of future Android devices?
        <em>
         Probably less than 10 years worth of SuperBowl spots.
        </em>
        Android developers take note.
       </p>
       <p>
        **E-Commerce On Android Pales In Comparison To iOS (
        <a href="http://bit.ly/hipmoblibrary">
         *Source: Hipmob Research
        </a>
        <em>
         )
        </em>
        *
        <img alt="" src="https://draftin.com:443/images/1416?token=e7wkDc0jcsEDkeUApCFiVTuOXPIijQwme5B_MTRtnS6xAHMJa-3pInSbfXRktF1__jSWDB72pJx0-zT2Dv6LUBg"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        In this world, if your brand has no reasonable expectation that users will buy your product online (careful here - your space might just not be disrupted
        <em>
         YET
        </em>
        ), Android is an unprecedented advertising opportunity. This makes even more sense if you consider that Google has architected Android to maximize reach. The OS is free, and any OEM can customize and release Android based devices. Why is it free? Because if more people have internet access, theyll search for more stuff, and Google, as the default search service, stands to benefit. Google can gather more data and sell more ads against those searches. This advantage is even more durable if you not only control the search service, but also the OS in which it lives.
        <strong>
         If Googles primary goal with Android is to sell more ads, why shouldnt yours be? Thats what its made for!
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         iOS Is Your Store: Shoppers Come There To Buy
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        For ecommerce businesses, iOS is already a force. In 6 short years, categories as varied as hotels,
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/53285549670/lessons-from-hautelook-what-happens-when-your">
         apparel
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/55874620762/wine-com-going-mobile-web-first">
         alcohol
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/61115721607/going-mobile-in-diapers-a-conversation-with-ron-and">
         family goods
        </a>
        have transitioned nearly half their revenues to mobile. Most of these sales come from iPhone or iPad users.
       </p>
       <p>
        Companies like
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/53285549670/lessons-from-hautelook-what-happens-when-your">
         HauteLook
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/55874620762/wine-com-going-mobile-web-first">
         Wine.com
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/61115721607/going-mobile-in-diapers-a-conversation-with-ron-and">
         Quidsi (the retailer behind Diapers.com)
        </a>
        show that its quite plausible to build a true commerce model on top of iOS, in a way that has yet to be true for Android. This is true for apps and the mobile web alike. Retailers like
        <a href="http://www.teacollection.com">
         Tea Collection
        </a>
        and
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/55874620762/wine-com-going-mobile-web-first">
         Wine.com
        </a>
        are seeing massive growth in mobile web sales, while folks like
        <a href="http://blog.hipmob.com/post/53285549670/lessons-from-hautelook-what-happens-when-your">
         HauteLook
        </a>
        and Gilt are seeing massive growth in mobile app sales. Most of this growth is coming from iOS - Ive yet to encounter a single e-commerce case where Android makes up more than 15% of mobile sales.
       </p>
       <p>
        In contrast to Android which monetizes with ads, the iOS and iPhone/iPad ecosystem
        <a href="http://ben-evans.com/benedictevans/2013/2/15/the-cheap-apple-phone-problem">
         profits (quite heartily) from from its users - they buy devices, accessories, and apps.
        </a>
        On the iOS App Store, you cant even download a free app without verifying your credit card! Thats right; in order to download a free app, you must first verify your payment credentials with Apple. Nuts.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="&lt;image&gt; iPhone/iPad margins graph &lt;/image&gt;" src="https://draftin.com:443/images/1409?token=kpmJzDHiROf39iSjUgyl4X9SVfuJYd5fx_oBwklqmKAi7QB45uPvh65oYimtzY53hhjPpXRGJLOmjEJJL5EfAgI"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        iOS has been built with massive trust in mind; Apple users buy more digital goods, buy more offline goods, Apple has every users credit card, and the nuances of the experiences built on iOS already support pure commerce. In addition, the empirical evidence points that way - at
        <a href="http://www.hipmob.com">
         Hipmob
        </a>
        weve talked to hundreds of brands and retailers, and interviewed several; every single one generates substantially more revenue from iOS than from Android.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Ecommerce Revenues On Android Pale Vs. iOS (
         <a href="http://bit.ly/hipmoblibrary">
          <em>
           Source: Hipmob Research
          </em>
         </a>
         )
        </strong>
        <img alt="" src="https://draftin.com:443/images/1577?token=1WrsALhF5_LqLGm5MDA7MxLIVH_gzdpPE63DAsMAJ9flshOQ4Me0sOBYzWRM_onvNnU6iHsjJ4edhK1PPFkrrIQ"/>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         33% of our traffic comes from iOS and 5% comes from Android.
        </em>
        -
        <a href="http://www.linkedin.com/pub/sarah-knup/1/744/674">
         Sarah Knup
        </a>
        , Head of Marketing &amp; Strategy,
        <a href="http://www.teacollection.com">
         Tea Collection
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Apple, with iOS,  has assembled a demographic that likes to buy online, and
        <a href="http://techcrunch.com/2013/09/23/apples-iphone-5s-and-iphone-5c-sell-9m-units-over-opening-weekend-topping-5m-for-iphone-5-last-year/">
         buys in growing numbers
        </a>
        . Android has assembled 3X as many activations as iOS. So what you do depends on your business. If your business depends on consumer advertising coupled with offline sales, then you should  weight your Android strategy towards getting more eyeballs and making your brand ubiquitous, whether that means an developing or sponsoring apps, mobile sites or landing pages, mobile ads, or anything else. This doesnt preclude advertising or brand/content marketing on iOS - with ~500m devices activated, it cant be ignored. This just means that if your goal is purely reach, Android gets you more  bang for your buck. If online sales are a large or fast growing part of your business then youll probably have to invest much more heavily in iOS. Your competitors already are, and theyre seeing real results.
       </p>
       <hr/>
       <p>
        <em>
         Working on mobile? So are we - drop us a line at ayo@hipmob.com, wed love to hear from you.
        </em>
       </p>
