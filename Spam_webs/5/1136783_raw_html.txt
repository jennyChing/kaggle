      <div class="story-intro">
       <p>
        <strong>
         <!-- google_ad_section_start(name=story_introduction, weight=high) -->
         GLASGOW, for the record, has just put on a great Commonwealth Games. The Gold Coast will no doubt stage an even better Games in 2018.
         <!-- google_ad_section_end(name=story_introduction) -->
        </strong>
       </p>
      </div>
      <!-- // .story-intro -->
      <!-- google_ad_section_start(name=story_body, weight=high) -->
      <p>
       If Glasgow was all kilts and bagpipes, the Gold Coast will be surf and sunshine. These events have a flavour which extends beyond the sporting field. They are enjoyable to watch for both sports-lovers and non sports-lovers. The question is, do enough of us still care?
      </p>
      <p>
       There are two really quick and foolproof ways to test the relevance of any sporting event. Firstly: is anyone willing to broadcast it? And secondly: is anyone interested enough to watch?
      </p>
      <p>
       The answer to the first question, for now, is clouded. Broadcast rights for the 2018 Commonwealth Games remain up for grabs. Channel Ten paid $30 million for the Glasgow Games, but has not yet stepped forward for the 2018 party on home soil. Neither has anybody else, although Commonwealth Games bosses expect bids after Glasgow.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w366h id1227012007695">
       <div class="module-content">
        <div class="image-block image-650w366h">
         <div class="image-frame">

         </div>
         <!--  // .image-frame -->
         <p class="caption">
          <span class="caption-text">
           The lack of interest in broadcast rights could be quite a blow for Gold Coast 2018 Games organisers. (AP Photo/Peter Morrison)
          </span>
          <span class="image-source">
           <em>
            Source:
           </em>
           AP
          </span>
         </p>
         <!-- // .caption -->
        </div>
        <!-- // .image-block -->
       </div>
       <!-- // .module-content -->
      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       As for the second question, yes, we are clearly still interested enough to watch. If you combine the figures of Ten and its little digital brother One, Ten has notched up some great numbers this week, often commanding 30 per cent or more of the prime time audience. Australians obviously still have an appetite for this event.
      </p>
      <p>
       But that may not last forever. To illustrate why, lets go to an average lounge room where an average Australian family is watching this thing. Youll excuse us if it happens to be this reporters lounge room and this reporters average Australian family.
      </p>
      <p>
       This reporter watched these Games with his primary school-aged children for an hour or so each evening between 7 and 8pm, then again in the morning at around the same time. The cat sat with us. We may or may not have spilled marmalade on the Ikea couch. (Dont worry sweetheart, well give that cushion a wash any day now.)
      </p>
      <p>
       It was interesting watching the Games with kids because in a sense, we all watch the Commonwealth Games as children. The kids see Australians competing  and usually excelling  in every event and think thats normal. We adults suspend our disbelief and pretend thats normal too. Aussie, Aussie, Aussie. Oi! Oi! Oi!
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227012005875">
       <div class="module-content">
        <div class="image-block image-650w488h">
         <div class="image-frame">

         </div>
         <!--  // .image-frame -->
         <p class="caption">
          <span class="caption-text">
           Ah, its so much fun beating up on global sporting superpowers like the Seychelles. (Photo by Hannah Peters/Getty Images)
          </span>

        <!-- // .image-block -->

       <!-- // .module-content -->
      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       But as we all know, this is not an accurate view of world sport. Australians do not make the final of everything in world sport. We do not win Olympic medals in boxing and ping pong and weightlifting, let alone gold medals. We go OK in the pool at the Olympics and World Championships, but not 19-gold-medals-OK. In fact, we won just one gold in the London pool.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sport has changed since the first Commonwealth Games in 1930 (or Empire Games as they were then). Everythings changed. Much of that change has occurred in the last 20 years or so.
      </p>
      <p>
       Back in 1982, when the adorable, winking giant kangaroo Matilda heralded the start of the Brisbane Commonwealth Games, we were still largely starved of big-time international sport on our TV screens. Any major contest between nations seemed important, even if only featured the 70 or so pink nations on the old world maps.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w488h id1227012007747">
       <div class="module-content">
        <div class="image-block image-650w488h">
         <div class="image-frame">

         </div>
         <!--  // .image-frame -->
         <p class="caption">
          <span class="caption-text">

       <!-- // .module-content -->
      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       These days we can watch everything from international darts to the English Premier League regularly on pay TV. International sportsmen and women also travel to us. You can turn up at a Big Bash game in cricket  or even a humble A-League game  and see some of the worlds best stars on our own doorstep, year in, year out.
      </p>
      <p>
       All of this makes an event like the Commonwealth Games much less relevant.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Commonwealth Games still have merit as a place for athletes to blood themselves in a Games environment which is just like the Olympics. They also provide a platform for lower-profile sports to showcase themselves and their stars. The diving this week, to cite just one example, was great. So was the hockey and netball.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
      <div class="module image-module module-image-650w1000h id1227012005955">
       <div class="module-content">
        <div class="image-block image-650w1000h">

         </div>
         <!--  // .image-frame -->
         <p class="caption">
          <span class="caption-text">

        <!-- // .image-block -->

       <!-- // .module-content -->
      </div>
      <!-- // .module .image-module .module-image-freeform -->
      <p>
       But has the action been world class? Well, occasionally, with the likes of the inimitable Sally Pearson, but mostly not. Has it been enjoyable? Absolutely. Its been great fun. Weve all loved it.
      </p>
      <p>
       But when you consider it costs the best part of a billion dollars to stage these things, and when you reflect on the fact that just two cities will bid for the 2022 Games  Edmonton in Canada (which staged the games in 1978) and Durban, South Africa  you can see theres not exactly a rush to be involved.
      </p>
      <p>
       Maybe its time to draw a line after 2022. To say OK, its been great but the world has changed, lets move on. Then again, perhaps we can maintain our wide-eyed childish enthusiasm and say hey, who cares if its not the best against the best? Its sport, its athletes giving their all and thatll do.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
