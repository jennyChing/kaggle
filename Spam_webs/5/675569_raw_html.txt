             <h1>
              The One Trick to Fixing Customer Service
             </h1>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
       </header>
       <div class="row">
        <div class="large-14 push-5 columns">
         <div class="padded-box popped-box plain-white pvs authored-content looser">
          <div class="at-above-post addthis-toolbox at-wordpress-hide" data-title="The One Trick to Fixing Customer Service" data-url="http://www.realbusiness.com/2014/08/customer-care/the-one-trick-to-fixing-customer-service/">
          </div>
          <div class="at-above-post-recommended addthis-toolbox at-wordpress-hide">
          </div>
          <p class="sub-header prominent h5">
           <time class="updated" datetime="2014-08-11" pubdate="">
            August 11, 2014
           </time>
           By Sachin Shenolikar
          </p>
          <p>
           It was the call heard round the Internet. Ryan Block, a former tech reporter,
           <a href="https://soundcloud.com/ryan-block-10/comcastic-service">
            posted a recording
           </a>
           of a phone conversation he had with a Comcast rep when trying to have his cable TV and Internet service discontinued.
          </p>
          <p>
           The rep kept trying to get Block and his wife to change their minds and stay on as customers. But the pushiness went too far (and for far too long). The result: a viral story that put Comcast in a very poor light.
          </p>
          <p>
           Blocks recording started a conversation about call center service protocol  and it soon became clear that the problems may go much deeper than some overzealous call reps.
          </p>
          <p>
           One person who knows the ins and outs of customer service is
           <a href="https://twitter.com/FrankEliason">
            Frank Eliason
           </a>
           . In 2008, Eliason
           <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/managing/content/jan2009/ca20090113_373506.htm">
            became a trailblazer
           </a>
           in social media customer service when he created the
           <a href="https://twitter.com/comcastcares">
            ComcastCares
           </a>
           Twitter handle. Now the director of social media for Citibank, Eliason has plenty of thoughts on how to right the system.
          </p>
          <p>
           He spoke with Real Business about the evolving landscape of customer service.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Innovation in call centers is said to be
           </b>
           <a href="http://www.wds.co/overcoming-innovation-phobia-contact-center/">
            <b>
             notoriously slow
            </b>
           </a>
           <b>
            . Why is that?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Its the efficiency mindset and a lack of justifying what their job is. Why do companies or call centers get caught up in trying to sell everybody something when they call? Its a means of justifying their existence. Were going to become the
           <i>
            sales
           </i>
           center.
          </p>
          <p>
           [Management] is not listening. [Theyre] not listening to our customers and not listening to our agents. I
           <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2014/7/28/5936959/comcast-confessions-when-every-call-is-a-sales-call">
            saw a quote
           </a>
           from a senior leader at Comcast saying, This is not what were about. But then they had quotes from employees that stated otherwise. Huge disconnect. That disconnect happens because theyre not hearing what that agent experience really is.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Is that the key takeaway  hearing the other side?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           The manager disconnect is probably the No. 1 issue. I have a funny line in a
           <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/today/post/article/20140731005911-19577932-dear-customer-service-industry">
            LinkedIn post
           </a>
           that says, Hey Im the customer  my eyes are up here!Stop looking at my wallet. Ultimately thats what service has become.Its been happening for years and continues to happen.
          </p>
          <p>
           There are very few providers that create outstanding experiences when you talk to them. There are some, dont get me wrong, but for the most part most call centers [dont provide] a good experience, and consumers dread it. I hate calling companies. I shouldnt have to hate calling them. If I need to call them I should want to.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            On that note, one of the bigger developments in customer service is the intelligent virtual agent. How do you see those changing the landscape?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Ive never been a fan of it. First of all, whats the message youresending to the customer? Youre not important  Im going to give youto a computer. Yes, you can do a lot more [with computers] than you could in the past, so theresless of a need to call. [But] you have to be careful what you wish for. Ifyour customer is never calling you or never contacting you, you no longerhave a relationship. Youre the same as any other provider out there.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            The argument is, you dont have to wait on hold anymore for a human whois talking to someone else, and the computer can handle thingsefficiently.
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Thats the argument. Wheres that argument coming from? That argumentis coming from, Customers are expensive  I dont want to have to put upwith them. You lose right off the bat. Its not staring off with
           <i>
            thecustomer is desiring this
           </i>
           . What the customer truly desires is A. To nothave a problem in the first place, and B. To have their problem resolvedfast and efficient. Thats really all they desire. Ultimately, could [virtual agents] get to the point where theyre good? Yes, but at thesame time what happens when theyre bad? What happens when they givea dumb answer?
          </p>
          <p>
           Were now entering a stage where humanity has to comeback into business. And it becomes about relationships. These thingsdont necessarily lend themselves to doing that.
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignnone" id="attachment_3794" style="width: 3112px">
           <img alt="Unknown" class=" wp-image-3794 colorbox-3788" height="2563" src="http://www.realbusiness.com/content/uploads/2014/08/Unknown.jpeg" title="Frank Eliason" width="3102"/>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Frank Eliason
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
           <b>
            Whats the current state of customer service over social media? How have things evolved since you got the ball rolling back a couple of years ago?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Last year I
           <a href="http://www.forbes.com/sites/xerox/2013/07/01/social-media-customer-service-is-a-failure/">
            wrote a post
           </a>
           that called social media customer service a failure, and its still a failure. If you study social media complaints, 90+ percent of the time the customer already tried to contact you and failed. So now theyll blast you on social media and get theyll help  and they usually do get help. So whats the message? Blast the brand on social media and then well help you. Thats not something you want long-term.
          </p>
          <p>
           In my view, social media customer service was always about helping someone who needs help, but also taking that information and fixing whats broken. What I see in the Comcast example, and this happens all the time, is things not being fixed.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            You dont see companies developing relationships with customers by having conversations with them on social media?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           No. The fact is, its always about complaints or failures, and it continues to be that.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            What can be done to improve it?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Its taking this information as youre gleaning it and repairing the problems  whether its in the process, the product, or any number of things. Its actually taking the information and doing something with it. Thats what we need to be doing. Service is an opportunity to learn something to make it better.
          </p>
          <p>
           <b>
            Looking ahead in the next three to five years, what are some things companies should keep in mind to improve their customer service, whether its over social media or phone?
           </b>
          </p>
          <p>
           Were going to see a dramatic effort to improve the customer experience overall. Companies are realizing that they do need to fix these things that are broken, and theyre starting to work on them. Were going to see the growth of things like chief customer officers, and theyre going to focus on experience across all channels.
          </p>
          <p>
           Theres also going to be a major transition in the way we think about service  to a broader more relationship-driven aspect, with the understanding that
           <i>
            I dont talk to customers much, but when I do, I want them to walk away feeling good about us
           </i>
           . The years ahead are going to be extraordinarily positive for customer experience people.
          </p>
