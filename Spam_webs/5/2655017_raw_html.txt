       <header class="entry-header">
        <h1 class="entry-title">
         Tips for Visiting Marrakech
        </h1>
        <div class="cheatyHide">
         <aside class="entry-meta">
          <span class="custom-meta entry-category">
           <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/category/features/" rel="category tag">
            Features
           </a>
           /
           <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/category/features/travel-tips/" rel="category tag">
            Travel Tips
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="custom-meta">
           <strong>
            dsq_thread_id:
           </strong>
           2737619097
          </span>
          <div class="clear">
          </div>
         </aside>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </div>
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header -->
       <div class="entry-content">
        <input class="jpibfi" type="hidden"/>
        <p>
         Marrakech is an enchanting, intoxicating place. It can also feel overwhelming and intimidating. Here are some tips I learned from experience, and that were shared with me, that I hope are helpfulto you when you visit.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Medina-Marrakech-1.jpg">
          <img alt="Medina, Marrakech-1" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-458" height="1280" src="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Medina-Marrakech-1.jpg" width="914"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Dress with care.
         </strong>
         Morocco is a Muslim country, so keep this in mind when dressing, particularly when inside the Medina.I put plenty of thought into what I could pack that would be comfortable in the heat, considerate of the locals, and still somewhat stylish  and embraced flowing skirts and maxi dresses. And yet, I was surprisedby the number of tourists I witnessed who showed complete disregard for this. Women (and not just young ones) were wearing hot pants, strapless tops with plenty of cleavage, and tiny, body hugging dresses. Its not a crime, but it is insensitive. As a general rule: Both women and men should cover their shoulders, and hemlines should be at least knee length for women.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Dont feel nervous about haggling in the souks
         </strong>
          it is expected! You can usually get sellers down to at least half the price they initially said. Do it with a smile, show interest in their wares and walk away if you cant agree on a price  there will be plenty more shops selling something similar.
         <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Medina-Marrakech-4.jpg">
          <img alt="Medina, Marrakech-4" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-455" height="961" src="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Medina-Marrakech-4.jpg" width="1280"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Try not tolook lost.
         </strong>
         We found that the second you consult a map, you will be accosted by locals (mainly young men) offering to show you the way. Our riadmanager warned us that this offer would more often than not include a surprise stop by one of their friends stores, and he was right. These boys will also expect a tip for their efforts. You are better to go into a shop and ask for directions, as the shopkeeper is not going to leave his store unattended. If you find yourself being followed by some very pushyyoung men insisting on showing you the way(as I did multiple times)give them a polite but firm, No, thank you. I also ended up walking into a shop, or turning and walking in the other direction, just to shake them off a few times. Having said that, the vast majority are very friendly and helpful without an ulterior motive.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          See Jemaa el Fna at different times
         </strong>
         . The main square completely transforms from day to night. Make sure you witness both. The square is surrounded by restaurants and cafes with roof terraces, so head up to one of these and watch the night markets being set up at sunset.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Jemaa-el-Fna-Marrakech-2.jpg">
          <img alt="Jemaa el Fna, Marrakech-2" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-490" height="961" src="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Jemaa-el-Fna-Marrakech-2.jpg" width="1280"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Expect to pay for pictures.
         </strong>
         Just like the gladiators outside the Colosseum in Rome, the snake charmers and monkey handlers in Jemaa el Fna will want money if you stop to take a picture.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Agree on a taxi fare before you get in
         </strong>
          nowhere in town should cost more than 50 dirhams. Arrange with your hotel or riad for a hotel transfer, as many of the taxis outside the airport will try to rip you off.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Get up early to go to Jardin Majorelle.
         </strong>
         The beautiful garden is one of the most popular tourist sights, and gets very crowded. It is best enjoyed, though, with few people around when you can appreciate the garden for the peaceful oasis it was intended to be. It opens daily at 8am, so be an earlybird and get there before the crowds descend  itll be worth it!
         <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Jardin-Majorelle.jpg">
          <img alt="Jardin Majorelle" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-488" height="961" src="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Jardin-Majorelle.jpg" width="1280"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Have plenty of small notes and coins for tips.
         </strong>
         Many taxi drivers and shopkeepers will try to tell you they dont have any change when you give them big notes, so having small money on hand will avoid this problem.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Want a cocktail? They can be hard to find.
         </strong>
         Mostof the restaurants in Marrakech are unlicensed. Some popular licensed eateries include
         <a href="http://www.cafearabe.com" target="_blank">
          Cafe Arabe
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="http://www.alfassia.com/restaurants-al-fassia-refined-moroccan-restaurants.html" target="_blank">
          Al Fassia
         </a>
         , and
         <a href="https://plus.google.com/105695424518342601668/about?gl=uk&amp;hl=en" target="_blank">
          Bo &amp; Zin
         </a>
         .
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Stay in a riad for a truly authentic experience.
         </strong>
         Hotels are great, but in most you could be anywhere in the world. There are plenty of luxury riad options if you still want hotel qualityservice and comfort, and many feel like an oasis of calm in an often hectic city. Also, make sure it has air conditioning! I stayed at
         <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/ptit-habibi-riad-marrakech/" target="_blank">
          Ptit Habibi
         </a>
         , and loved it.
         <a href="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Riad-Ptit-Habibi-Marrakech.jpg">
          <img alt="Riad P'tit Habibi, Marrakech" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-489" height="1280" src="http://www.somekindofwanderlust.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/Riad-Ptit-Habibi-Marrakech.jpg" width="961"/>
         </a>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Wear comfy shoes.
         </strong>
         Particularly in the Medina, the ground is cobbled or dirt  neither of which is ideal in heels. A good pair of sandals or trainers are perfect for wandering.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Try the orange juice and food from the markets in Jemma el Fna.
         </strong>
         Its delicious and great value  use your hands for eating, as the locals do, as the utensils are often not washed properly.Anti-bacterial wipes are handy to have in your purse.
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          French and Arabic are the most widely spoken languages.
         </strong>
         Arabic may beyond most of our language abilities, but knowing some basic French phrases is both helpful and thoughtful. I always download a translator app with basic phrases before visiting a foreign country  it has been a lifesaver more times than I can count!
        </p>
        <p>
        </p>
        <p>
         Anything I have missed? What are your top tips for Marrakech?
        </p>
