      <div class="paragraphs col-md-8" id="article__body">
       <span itemprop="body">
        <div class="content parsys">
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Every summer, Caitlin OConnell, author of
           <a href="http://www.press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo17768032.html">
            <i>
             Elephant Don: The Politics of a Pachyderm Posse
            </i>
           </a>
           ,packs her bags and travels to northern Namibia to study a group of male elephants. What she witnesses as the males jockey for power and position around a water hole is both shocking and heart-warming: violent conflicts, tender scenes of affection. But, above all, the elephants show her the importance of family and ritual behavior.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Talking from her home in San Diego, she explains what a smelly T-shirt contest can teach us about elephants, why it is no easier being a teenage elephant than a human one, what she did when she discovered a deadly, black mamba snake behind a canvas wall at the camp, and why it is crucial for humans and elephants to learn to live together.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            Your book is based on fieldwork in a place called Mushara, a little known part of Namibia. Put us on the ground.
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Our field site is in
           <a href="http://etoshanationalpark.co.za">
            Etosha National Park
           </a>
           , Namibia. Its a remote area of the park where there are very few water points. Its great for us because the elephants have to congregate at this particular site and we dont have traffic from tourism. We have a seven-meter observation tower, with viewing platforms and tents, where we set up camp for the month of July. Thats when elephants concentrate in the area, and we can see the soap opera unfold.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text parbase PullQuote section">
          <div class="pull-quote small left ">
           <blockquote class="double">
            <p>
             The way male elephants interact with each other is very similar to a ritual society, like the Mafioso.
            </p>
           </blockquote>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="parbase Image image section">
          <figure class="image media--xsmall left " itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
           <div class="news-image-wrap " style="padding-bottom: 150.03663003663004%">
            <div class="delayed-image-load" data-alt="Picture of the cover of Elephant Don by Caitlin O'Connell" data-itemprop="contentUrl" data-src="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/content/dam/news/2015/04/17/booktalk/02elephantbooktalk.ngsversion.af107a2aae87b363d1d35bd9ca362ed8.jpg/{width}/{pixel_ratio}">
            </div>
           </div>
          </figure>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            You use the term Don to describe one of the male elephants you study. Tell us about Gregand why he is the
            <i>
             capo dei capi
            </i>
            ?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The way male elephants interact with each other is very similar to a ritual society, like the Mafioso. A subordinate elephant will take its trunk, lift it up and place it in Gregs mouth. Thats why I call him The Don. Theres this reverence around him, but also a brutality. He has figured out how to wield the carrot and stick to keep his constituency. Ive seen very aggressive bulls that are not able to hold the kind of posse he does. Theyre too aggressive and individuals arent interested in following them. But he solicits the subordinate individuals to follow him. Its a fascinating, secret society.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            How old is Greg? Describe him for us.
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           We estimate hes in his mid-forties. Hes not the largest bull there, but dominance has more to do with character than physicality, although you have to be very fit to challenge other bulls. He doesnt have the biggest tusks. In physical appearance he wouldnt stand out as the most impressive bull at our field site. But your eye is drawn to him because of how he holds himself and how others view him. Its like theres a spotlight on him.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="section RightRailSlot">
          <div class="right-rail-slot align-upper-paragraph" style="margin-top:0.0px">
           <div class="parsys rightRailContent">
            <div class="OneColumn section PromoRelatedContent Promo">
             <div class="related-content-promo article__square_block left">
              <header>
               <h6 class="title">
               </h6>
               <p>
                Related Content
               </p>
              </header>
              <div class="square_body">
               <ol>
                <li class="helvetica-before clearfix">
                 <span itemprop="relatedLink">
                  <a href="http://www.nationalgeographic.com/news-features/what-elephant-calls-mean/" itemprop="url" target="_blank">
                   What Elephant Calls Mean: A User's Guide
                  </a>
                 </span>
                </li>
                <li class="helvetica-before clearfix">
                 <span itemprop="relatedLink">
                  <a href="http://voices.nationalgeographic.com/2013/04/24/elephants-communicate-in-sophisticated-sign-language-researchers-say/" itemprop="url" target="_blank">
                   Elephants Communicate in Sophisticated Sign Language, Researchers Say
                  </a>
                 </span>
                </li>
               </ol>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            The male hierarchy is all about who gets the best water, right?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           When one social animal has dominance over a resource, you assume that theres dominance over others. At the water hole, its like the ritual lining up and kissing of the Mafioso ring.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           There is one spot at the water hole that is the source of a spring. Elephants are very particular about their drinking water and they fight over access to the best water. So that spot is reserved for the Don. When he comes in, its like the parting of the waters. The other males step away so that he can have that spot without contest. Others jockeying for position will ask his permission to drink by putting their trunk in his. Individuals that are low ranking dont even bother going to the best water. They go straight to the more salty water at the end of the pen. [Laughs]
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="parbase Image image section">
          <div class="fullsize-element ">
           <div class="media__medium--container container">
            <div class="row">
             <div class="col-md-13">
              <figure class="image media--medium left " itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
               <div class="news-image-wrap " style="padding-bottom: 65.91796875%">

                </div>
               </div>
               <figcaption class="media__caption medium-caption">
                <div class="media__caption--text">

               </figcaption>
              </figure>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            It sounds like the struggle for hierarchy can be pretty violent among males.
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Oh, yes! They know how to use those tusks. Even if they dont have big tusks, they really clash. It doesnt happen very often, and the clashes are mostly in relation to two musth males competing for a female. You can hear their heads clashing and the tusks clacking, and see how violently those tusks can jab. If youre off center, youre leaving your flank vulnerable to being stabbed by a huge tusk. So, they will square off like sumo wrestlers, and never turn their backs. If they do, they immediately run. Its an amazing thing to
           <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mOH3ZQCl-8k">
            watch
           </a>
           .
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            You have used the term musth several times. What is it and how does it affect male elephant behavior?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Musth is an Indian word. One definition is "drunk." An elephant goes into a state of elevated testosterone, similar to rutting in deer or antelope. But elephants are unique in that they go into this state serially, not altogether, so theres a turn-taking element when some elephants are in musth and others arent.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           They have very specific behaviors that signal they are in musth. They dribble urine and have swollen temple glands which secrete a sticky fluid. They take their trunks and swing them across their face, smearing themselves with this smelly substance. They prance and wave their ears and curl their trunks. Its quite a spectacle [Laughs].
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text parbase PullQuote section">
          <div class="pull-quote small left ">
           <blockquote class="double">
            <p>
             Like the teen male, elephants have a coming of age period, with testosterone spikes and oscillations.
            </p>
           </blockquote>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Its as though they are on stage and its their turn to mate with females. Other males will back down, except for those overlapping in their musth period. Then they will challenge each other. Something thats very unconventional here is that Greg, the dominant bull of what I call The Boys Club at Mashara, appears to be able to suppress others within his group from going into musth. Weve never seen that before. So were hoping to shed some new light on musth.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="parbase Image image section">
          <div class="fullsize-element ">
           <div class="media__medium--container container">
            <div class="row">
             <div class="col-md-13">
              <figure class="image media--medium left " itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
               <div class="news-image-wrap " style="padding-bottom: 64.013671875%">
                <div class="delayed-image-load" data-alt="Picture of an elephant at a watering hole in Namibia" data-itemprop="contentUrl" data-src="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/content/dam/news/2015/04/17/booktalk/04elephantbooktalk.ngsversion.2cc7878b8c65b410bc0ea8f7b6671121.jpg/{width}/{pixel_ratio}">
                </div>
               </div>
               <figcaption class="media__caption medium-caption">
                <div class="media__caption--text">
                 <span itemprop="caption">
                  <p>
                   A bull elephant at a water hole in Etosha National Park. Males jockey for position and fight for access to the best water.
                  </p>
                 </span>
                 <br/>
                 <small class="media__caption--credit" itemprop="author">
                  Photograph by Alex Saberi, National Geographic Creative
                 </small>
                </div>
               </figcaption>
              </figure>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            Being a teenager is no easier for elephants than it is for humans, is it?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           No [Laughs]. Like the teen male, elephants have a coming of age period, with testosterone spikes and oscillations. Older males try and put these youngsters in their place, so theyre constantly getting harassed. Its a very emotional time. Its like theyre getting their drivers license. They want to be free from their family but they still want to come home at night [Laughs]. So there are two things pulling at these young bulls.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Once they do make that break with family, they are all alone, and have to find a new family that will accept then. Some of them band together and solicit support from older individuals. Not all older individuals are interested in adopting them. But some are, and Greg is one of the great ones. He will, literally, take them under his wing. He will take his head and put his ear over them and rub them.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            You deploy a lot of human behavioral tests on your subjects. Tell us about the
            <a href="http://www.pbs.org/wgbh/evolution/library/01/6/l_016_08.html">
             smelly T-shirt contest
            </a>
            and how it relates to elephants.
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="section RightRailSlot">
          <div class="right-rail-slot align-upper-paragraph" style="margin-top:0.0px">
           <div class="parsys rightRailContent">
            <div class="OneColumn section PromoRelatedContent Promo">
             <div class="related-content-promo article__square_block left">
              <header>
               <h6 class="title">
               </h6>
               <p>
                Related Content
               </p>
              </header>
              <div class="square_body">
               <ol>
                <li class="helvetica-before clearfix">
                 <span itemprop="relatedLink">
                  <a href="http://voices.nationalgeographic.com/blog/a-voice-for-elephants/" itemprop="url" target="_blank">
                   Find Out More at National Geographic's Elephant Blog
                  </a>
                 </span>
                </li>
               </ol>
              </div>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           [Laughs] Its a very clever study that a group of Swedish scientists did to show that humans are just as subject to olfactory decisions as other species. They had different men exercise in T-shirts. Then they put those T-shirts in a box and had women at different stages of their ovulation cycle smell them to see which one they preferred. What theyre actually smelling is whats called a major
           <a href="http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/359034/major-histocompatibility-complex-MHC">
            histocompatability
           </a>
           complex. Its a gene related to our immune system. Its also connected to what would be called a pheromone: our individual perfume.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The theory is that women select smells that are not so foreign from herself, but yet not so familiar. The not so familiar part is to prevent any species from mating too close to their kin, like mating with your first cousin. A lot of different species have this to protect them from inbreeding, but also to protect them from mating too far outside their gene pool.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text parbase PullQuote section">
          <div class="pull-quote small left ">
           <blockquote class="double">
            <p>
             Despite the fact that these animals have a trunk and look different from us, they show us how important ritual and family are.
            </p>
           </blockquote>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           I was curious whether it applied to this group that Greg had formed with his male posse. So, we did the genetics on their fecal samples, and the results were surprising. It turned out that they were more related to each other than the individuals I tested from outside that group. This is something wed also like to test on long-term memory with elephants in captivity, looking at recognition of a trainer or a mate that they havent been with for many years.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="parbase Image image section">
          <div class="fullsize-element ">
           <div class="media__medium--container container">
            <div class="row">
             <div class="col-md-13">
              <figure class="image media--medium left " itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject">
               <div class="news-image-wrap " style="padding-bottom: 67.3828125%">
                <div class="delayed-image-load" data-alt="Picture of an elephant throwing dust at sunset" data-itemprop="contentUrl" data-src="http://news.nationalgeographic.com/content/dam/news/2015/04/17/booktalk/05elephantbooktalk.ngsversion.28d30e5b523f7d087cdd8514c3ab662b.jpg/{width}/{pixel_ratio}">
                </div>
               </div>
               <figcaption class="media__caption medium-caption">
                <div class="media__caption--text">
                 <span itemprop="caption">
                  <p>
                   An elephant tosses dust on its hide as protection against the African sun on the flats of the Etosha Pan, a prehistoric lake bed in Etosha National Park.
                  </p>
                 </span>
                 <br/>
                 <small class="media__caption--credit" itemprop="author">
                  Photograph by Annie Griffiths, National Geographic Creative
                 </small>
                </div>
               </figcaption>
              </figure>
             </div>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            You are also surrounded by lions in the field. Is it as wonderful as it sounds?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           Oh, it is so wonderful! I feel so lucky to be able to have this experience, being out there in the wild every season, reminded of my place in nature and the universe. Listening to lions roar every nightfrom a position of safety, of course [laughs]is a real privilege. The night sounds are otherworldly, so is the night sky in Namibia. Its so brilliant at night. It feels like you can reach out from the tower and touch the Southern Cross.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            Your field station also has some serious dangers. Tell us about
            <a href="http://animals.nationalgeographic.com/animals/reptiles/black-mamba/">
             black mambas
            </a>
            .
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           The black mamba has a formidable reputation for a reason. Its an incredibly aggressive, poisonous snake. A friend of a friend had his arm out the window as he was driving in a Land Rover, and this mamba reared up on the side of the road and bit him on the elbow!
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           We had one in camp once. Everyone was in the tower watching elephants, my husband, Tim, and I were down by the door of the camp and he heard some rustling behind the canvas wall. He said, Caitlin, I think I just heard a snake. So we stepped over and there indeed was a snake sitting in the shade behind the canvas door! It was a younger one, with that distinctive dark olive color, small head and black, beady eyes. Its called the black mamba because the inside of the mouth is black.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           We looked at each other and thought: Oh my god, what are we going to do? Luckily, there were some two meter-long pieces of PVC for the electrical fencing. So Tim said, Lets take that, run a string through it and make a noose. So we made two of these long nooses and erected some tables as shields. As we moved closer, we placed the two nooses on top of the mambas neck, and pulled!
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            Most of us assume that poaching is the greatest threat to elephants. But human-elephant conflict is also important. Just as no one wants a highway built near their house,
            <a href="http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/nimbyism">
             NIMBYism
            </a>
            plays a role here, doesnt it?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           I think there are three things at play for elephants. One is the urgent poaching crisis. Because I worked with farmers on elephant-human conflict mitigation, Im also keenly aware of two other problems. One is habitat loss. If we make cornfields instead of elephant habitat, were going to say goodbye to the elephant. Third is elephant-human conflict. If elephants are trampling a subsistence farmers crops, eating their whole years worth of food in one night, you cant blame them for not wanting them in their backyard. So, we have to help people mitigate conflict so that its easier for people to share space with elephants.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <b>
            What do you love about working with elephants? And what can they teach us?
           </b>
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           I love watching ritual behaviors between elephants, seeing how sensitive and caring they are about each other, like when two females kneel down and pull a baby out of the mud. Despite the fact that these animals have a trunk and look different from us, they show us how important ritual and family are. Even shaking someones hand and looking them in the eye is an important part of the ritual of interacting.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           When you see elephants behaving terribly to each other, its also a reminder. Humans can treat each other terribly, too. But cant we rise above that? Were not so driven by our environment that we are competing for resources in the same way. Its a reminder to look in the mirror and try to be better.
          </p>
         </div>
         <div class="text smartbody parbase section">
          <p>
           <i>
            This interview has been edited and condensed.
           </i>
          </p>
